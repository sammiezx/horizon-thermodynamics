Jacobson 1995 — step-by-step Q&A guide

Thermodynamics of Spacetime: The Einstein Equation of State. Phys. Rev. Lett. 75, 1260 (1995). gr-qc/9504004.
Each section: one question, a brief hint to think on, an answer, sometimes a problem. Read in order.

Phase 0Orientation

Q0.1 What does this paper actually claim?

Hint: look at the title. "Thermodynamics of Spacetime: The Einstein Equation of State."

Einstein's equation of general relativity can be derived by demanding that the first law of thermodynamics ($\delta Q = T \, dS$) hold for local horizons, assuming the entropy of any horizon is $S = A/(4 G \hbar)$.

The phrase "equation of state" is the key: Jacobson is suggesting GR is a thermodynamic identity, not a fundamental dynamical law — like the ideal gas law $PV = NkT$ relative to molecular mechanics.

Q0.2 Why is that surprising?

Hint: what would change if $PV = NkT$ were treated as a "fundamental law" rather than as a thermodynamic consequence?

$PV = NkT$ is not fundamental. It is what you get when you do statistical mechanics on $N \to \infty$ classical particles in a box. Pressure and temperature are coarse-grained macroscopic averages — they only make sense in the thermodynamic limit. You don't try to quantize $P$ or $T$ as fundamental operators.

If Einstein's equation is the same kind of thing, then $g_{\mu\nu}$ — the spacetime metric — is a coarse-grained variable, and quantizing it directly (canonical quantum gravity, loop quantum gravity, etc.) is suspect. The right quantum gravity would be a quantum theory of the underlying microstates, with GR emerging as the $N \to \infty$ limit.

That is the implication packed into the closing sentence of Jacobson 2003 §1.

Phase 1Thermodynamics you need (six concepts)

Q1.1 What is entropy?

Hint: two equivalent definitions, one statistical (Boltzmann), one thermodynamic (Clausius).

Boltzmann (statistical):

$$ S = k_B \ln \Omega $$

where $\Omega$ = number of microstates compatible with the macroscopic state.

Clausius (thermodynamic):

$$ dS = \frac{\delta Q_{\rm rev}}{T} $$

for reversible heat flow at temperature $T$. Stat-mech derives Clausius from Boltzmann.

Problem 1.1 A gas of $N$ molecules confined to the left half of a box (volume $V$) is freed (partition removed; total volume $2V$). What is $\Delta S$?
Solution Each molecule now has access to twice as many microstates. So $\Omega_{\rm after}/\Omega_{\rm before} = 2^N$, and $$\Delta S = k_B \ln(2^N) = N k_B \ln 2\,.$$

Q1.2 What is temperature, formally?

Hint: not "how hot." Express it as a partial derivative.

$$ \frac{1}{T} = \left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial U}\right)_{V, N} \;\Longleftrightarrow\; T = \left(\frac{\partial U}{\partial S}\right)_{V, N} $$

Temperature is the energy cost per unit increase in entropy. "Hot" things have high $T$ because adding energy to them produces relatively little new entropy.

For an ideal gas, $\langle KE \rangle = \tfrac{3}{2} k_B T$ — but that's a consequence, not the definition. The definition above generalizes to systems where "kinetic energy of molecules" isn't even well-defined (e.g., horizons).

Problem 1.2 Two systems A and B are in thermal contact, isolated from the outside. Prove that at equilibrium $T_A = T_B$.
Solution Total energy $U = U_A + U_B$ is conserved; total entropy $S = S_A(U_A) + S_B(U_B)$ is maximized. $$\frac{dS}{dU_A} = \frac{\partial S_A}{\partial U_A} - \frac{\partial S_B}{\partial U_B} = 0$$ $$\Longrightarrow \frac{1}{T_A} = \frac{1}{T_B} \Longrightarrow T_A = T_B\,.$$

Q1.3 The first law in the form Jacobson uses

Hint: for a heat transfer with no work done, $dU = \delta Q + \delta W$ reduces to what?

For reversible heat flow with no work:

$$ \boxed{\;\; \delta Q = T \cdot dS \;\;}$$

This is the equation Jacobson actually applies. Memorize it.

Q1.4 $\delta Q$ vs $dS$ — what's the notation hinting at?

Hint: $d$ and $\delta$ are different letters on purpose.

$dS$ is an exact differential: $S$ is a state function. $\int dS$ from state $A$ to state $B$ equals $S_B - S_A$, regardless of path.

$\delta Q$ is an inexact differential: $Q$ is a path function. $\int \delta Q$ depends on the route through state space, not just on endpoints.

The notation reminds you which is which. A path = something you traverse as time passes; $\delta Q$ inherits a time-direction from the path. $dS$ does not — it's a timeless property of the state.

Q1.5 What does "reversible" mean, and why does it matter?

Hint: compare slow isothermal expansion against Joule free expansion.

A process is reversible if at every infinitesimal step, the system is in equilibrium with its surroundings — so the process can be reversed by an infinitesimal change in conditions. Quasi-static, frictionless, no dissipation.

For reversible heat flow:

$$ \delta Q_{\rm rev} = T \cdot dS $$

For irreversible processes — Clausius inequality:

$$ \delta Q \;<\; T \cdot dS $$

The difference $T \cdot dS - \delta Q$ is entropy created by the irreversibility itself. This is where the arrow of time enters thermodynamics.

Problem 1.5 Compute $\Delta S$ for the same final state reached by (a) reversible isothermal expansion from $V \to 2V$ at temperature $T$, and (b) free expansion into vacuum (Joule expansion).
Solution For path (a): $\delta Q_{\rm rev} = P\,dV = (NkT/V)\,dV$, so $$Q_a = NkT \ln 2, \quad \Delta S_a = Q_a/T = Nk \ln 2\,.$$ For path (b): $W = 0$ (vacuum), $Q = 0$ (adiabatic), so $\Delta U = 0$, $T$ unchanged. But $\Delta S$ is a state function — same endpoints means same $\Delta S$: $$\Delta S_b = Nk \ln 2\,.$$ Yet $\int \delta Q/T = 0$ along path (b), strictly less than $\Delta S$. The entropy $Nk \ln 2$ was "created" by the irreversibility of free expansion.

Q1.6 What is an "equation of state"?

Hint: $PV = NkT$. What kind of equation is that — dynamical, structural, or something else?

A relation between state variables. Example: $PV = NkT$. Given any three of $(P, V, N, T)$, the fourth is fixed.

Crucial features:

  • Not an equation of motion. It doesn't say how the system evolves. It says what relationships hold at any moment.
  • Derived from statistical mechanics by taking the thermodynamic limit $N \to \infty$ over an underlying microscopic theory.
  • The macroscopic variables ($P$, $T$) are not fundamental — they're statistical averages over microstates.

Jacobson's claim: Einstein's equation is the same kind of object. Equation of state, not equation of motion. Derived, not fundamental.

Phase 2Physics you need (seven concepts)

Q2.1 What is a horizon?

Hint: a one-way membrane in spacetime. Three flavors are relevant here.

A boundary beyond which signals cannot causally reach the other side. Three flavors:

  • Event horizon (global, black-hole-flavored): defined by the entire future of spacetime.
  • Rindler horizon (flat space, accelerated observer): a 45° null line in a spacetime diagram. The horizon belongs to the observer, not the spacetime.
  • Local Rindler horizon (Jacobson's construction): at any point $p$ in any spacetime, the equivalence principle lets you construct a small piece of null surface through $p$ that acts as a Rindler-like horizon.

Jacobson uses the third kind.

Q2.2 What is a local Rindler horizon, more precisely?

Hint: how can a horizon be defined at a single point, when usually horizons are global?

Pick a point $p$. Pick a null direction $k^\mu$ at $p$. The null surface tangent to $k^\mu$ through $p$ is the local Rindler horizon at $p$.

$p$ itself lies on the horizon. The "small patch" is the section of this null surface in a tiny neighborhood of $p$. The construction is available at every point of every spacetime — only the equivalence principle is required.

No physical accelerating observer is needed. The horizon is a geometric object; the "observer" is just conceptual scaffolding to motivate why it has a temperature (next question).

Q2.3 Why does a horizon have a temperature?

Hint: Unruh effect (1976).

A uniformly accelerating observer with proper acceleration $a$ perceives the Minkowski vacuum as a thermal bath at temperature:

$$ T_{\rm Unruh} = \frac{\hbar a}{2\pi k_B c}\,. $$

This is a result of flat-space QFT (no GR). For a horizon with surface gravity $\kappa$:

$$ T = \frac{\hbar \kappa}{2\pi k_B}\,. $$

Jacobson takes this as input — he doesn't re-derive it.

Q2.4 Why is entropy proportional to area?

Hint: Bekenstein (1972), Hawking (1975).

For a black hole horizon of area $A$:

$$ S = \frac{k_B c^3 A}{4 G \hbar} \;=\; \frac{k_B A}{4 \ell_P^2},\qquad \ell_P^2 = G\hbar/c^3\,. $$

One bit of entropy per Planck-sized area patch. Extraordinary because for ordinary matter, entropy scales with volume, not area.

Jacobson's key move is to assume $S = \eta A$ with $\eta = 1/(4G\hbar)$ for any local horizon, not just black holes. This is the one place GR-flavored information enters the argument — and it enters as an assumption.

Q2.5 Null vectors, null surfaces, null generators

Hint: a vector $v$ is null if $g(v,v) = 0$.

Null vector: $v^\mu$ with $g_{\mu\nu} v^\mu v^\nu = 0$. Moves at the speed of light. In Minkowski with signature $(-,+,+,+)$, e.g.\ $v = (1, 1, 0, 0)$ is null since $-1 + 1 = 0$.

Null surface: a surface whose normal vector is null. Equivalently, a surface generated by light rays (null geodesics). Weird fact: the normal lies within the tangent space — the light-ray generator is both tangent and perpendicular to the surface.

Null generator: a null geodesic lying entirely within a null surface. Its tangent vector is null and along the surface.

Jacobson's $k^\mu$ is the null generator of the local Rindler horizon at $p$. Affine parameter $\lambda$ runs along it.

Q2.6 Killing vectors and the boost Killing vector

Hint: Killing vectors generate symmetries of the metric.

A Killing vector $\xi^\mu$ satisfies:

$$ \nabla_\mu \xi_\nu + \nabla_\nu \xi_\mu = 0\,. $$

Translation: moving along $\xi^\mu$ leaves the metric invariant. Each Killing vector produces a conserved quantity along geodesics: $E = -p_\mu \xi^\mu$.

For the local Rindler horizon at $p$, the relevant Killing vector (locally) is the boost Killing vector:

$$ \chi^\mu = \kappa \lambda\, k^\mu\,. $$

The conserved energy with respect to $\chi^\mu$ is the "boost energy" — the natural notion of energy for an accelerated observer.

Q2.7 The Raychaudhuri equation

Hint: how does the cross-sectional area of a bundle of light rays evolve?

For a null congruence (a family of null geodesics), define the expansion:

$$ \theta = \frac{1}{A}\, \frac{dA}{d\lambda} $$

($\theta > 0$ = bundle spreading; $\theta < 0$ = bundle focusing.) Raychaudhuri:

$$ \frac{d\theta}{d\lambda} = -\tfrac{1}{2}\theta^2 - \sigma^2 - R_{\mu\nu} k^\mu k^\nu $$

where $\sigma$ is the shear. Matter, via $R_{\mu\nu} k^\mu k^\nu$, focuses light rays — this is the geometric content of attractive gravity for null trajectories.

At a horizon's bifurcation point (where $\theta = \sigma = 0$):

$$ \frac{d\theta}{d\lambda} \approx -R_{\mu\nu} k^\mu k^\nu,\quad \theta(\lambda) \approx -R_{\mu\nu} k^\mu k^\nu \cdot \lambda $$

And the integrated area change is:

$$ \delta A \;\approx\; \int \theta\, d\lambda\, dA \;=\; -\int R_{\mu\nu} k^\mu k^\nu \cdot \lambda\, d\lambda\, dA $$

Phase 3The argument (six steps)

Q3.1 How does heat flow across the local horizon?

Hint: heat = energy flux of matter, measured with respect to the boost Killing vector.

$$ \delta Q \;=\; \int T_{\mu\nu}\, \chi^\mu\, d\Sigma^\nu $$

On the horizon, $d\Sigma^\nu = k^\nu d\lambda\, dA$ and $\chi^\mu = \kappa\lambda k^\mu$, so:

$$ \delta Q \;=\; \kappa \int T_{\mu\nu}\, k^\mu k^\nu\, \lambda\, d\lambda\, dA $$

The factor $\kappa\lambda$ is the boost weight that converts the raw matter flux into Killing energy.

Q3.2 How does the horizon area change?

Hint: Raychaudhuri from Q2.7.

$$ \delta A \;=\; -\int R_{\mu\nu}\, k^\mu k^\nu\, \lambda\, d\lambda\, dA $$

Therefore the entropy change is:

$$ \delta S \;=\; \eta\, \delta A \;=\; -\frac{1}{4 G \hbar} \int R_{\mu\nu}\, k^\mu k^\nu\, \lambda\, d\lambda\, dA $$

Q3.3 Impose the first law $\delta Q = T \cdot dS$

Hint: substitute, cancel $\kappa$, simplify constants.

Set $\delta Q$ from Q3.1 equal to $T \cdot \delta S$ where $T = \hbar\kappa/(2\pi)$:

$$ \kappa \int T_{\mu\nu} k^\mu k^\nu \lambda\, d\lambda\, dA \;=\; \frac{\hbar \kappa}{2\pi}\cdot\frac{1}{4G\hbar} \int R_{\mu\nu} k^\mu k^\nu \lambda\, d\lambda\, dA $$

Constants: $(\hbar\kappa/2\pi)(1/4G\hbar) = \kappa/(8\pi G)$. Divide both sides by $\kappa$:

$$ \int T_{\mu\nu} k^\mu k^\nu \lambda\, d\lambda\, dA \;=\; \frac{1}{8\pi G} \int R_{\mu\nu} k^\mu k^\nu \lambda\, d\lambda\, dA $$

The integrals match for every choice of null $k^\mu$. So the integrands agree pointwise:

$$ \boxed{\;\; T_{\mu\nu} k^\mu k^\nu \;=\; \frac{1}{8\pi G}\, R_{\mu\nu} k^\mu k^\nu \quad \forall\ \text{null } k^\mu \;\;} $$

Q3.4 From null $k^\mu$ to a full tensor equation

Hint: $g_{\mu\nu} k^\mu k^\nu = 0$ for null $k^\mu$. What does that let you add?

Two symmetric tensors $A_{\mu\nu}$ and $B_{\mu\nu}$ that agree when contracted on all null $k^\mu$ may still differ by a multiple of $g_{\mu\nu}$ — because $g_{\mu\nu} k^\mu k^\nu = 0$ is invisible to the null-$k$ constraint. So:

$$ T_{\mu\nu} \;=\; \frac{1}{8\pi G}\,\bigl( R_{\mu\nu} + f\, g_{\mu\nu} \bigr) $$

for some scalar $f$ to be determined.

Q3.5 Where does the cosmological constant Λ come from?

Hint: local energy conservation $\nabla^\mu T_{\mu\nu} = 0$ and the contracted Bianchi identity.

Apply $\nabla^\mu T_{\mu\nu} = 0$ to both sides of the expression for $T_{\mu\nu}$:

$$ 0 \;=\; \nabla^\mu T_{\mu\nu} \;=\; \frac{1}{8\pi G}\bigl(\nabla^\mu R_{\mu\nu} + \nabla_\nu f\bigr) $$

Contracted Bianchi: $\nabla^\mu R_{\mu\nu} = \tfrac{1}{2} \nabla_\nu R$. So:

$$ \nabla_\nu\!\left(\tfrac{1}{2} R + f\right) \;=\; 0 $$

Hence $\tfrac{1}{2} R + f$ is a constant. Call it $\Lambda$:

$$ f \;=\; -\tfrac{1}{2} R + \Lambda $$

The cosmological constant $\Lambda$ appears as an integration constant. Jacobson did not put it in by hand — it falls out for free.

Q3.6 Final result

Hint: substitute $f$ back.

From Q3.4 with $f = -R/2 + \Lambda$:

$$ T_{\mu\nu} \;=\; \frac{1}{8\pi G}\bigl(R_{\mu\nu} - \tfrac{1}{2} R\, g_{\mu\nu} + \Lambda\, g_{\mu\nu}\bigr) $$

Rearrange:

$$ \boxed{\;\; R_{\mu\nu} - \tfrac{1}{2} R\, g_{\mu\nu} + \Lambda\, g_{\mu\nu} \;=\; 8\pi G\, T_{\mu\nu} \;\;} $$

Einstein's equation — derived from thermodynamics + Unruh + entropy=area.

Phase 4The punchline

Q4.1 What does it mean that Einstein eq is "thermodynamic"?

Hint: compare side by side with the ideal gas law.

IDEAL GAS:
  microscopic:     N classical molecules with positions, velocities
  stat mech:       define P, T as averages over microstates
  thermo limit:    N → ∞
  result:          P V = N k_B T   (ideal gas law)

SPACETIME (Jacobson's conjecture):
  microscopic:     ??? — unknown microstates of "spacetime stuff"
  stat mech:       define  g_{μν}  as an average over microstates
  thermo limit:    N → ∞
  result:          R_{μν} − ½ R g_{μν} + Λg_{μν} = 8πG T_{μν}
                   (Einstein equation)

The arrows match exactly. Einstein's equation is, by analogy, an equation of state — a thermodynamic consequence of an underlying microscopic theory of spacetime that we have not yet identified.

Q4.2 What's the implication for quantum gravity?

Hint: you don't quantize $P$ or $T$ to find the quantum theory of an ideal gas.

If $g_{\mu\nu}$ is a thermodynamic-limit variable (like $P$ or $T$), then trying to quantize it directly — canonical quantum gravity, loop quantum gravity, perturbative quantization of GR — is suspect. You'd be quantizing the wrong variable.

The right quantum theory of gravity should be a quantum theory of the underlying microstates, with GR emerging as the $N \to \infty$ thermodynamic limit. The unknown "underlying microstates" are the central question of modern theoretical physics. Candidates:

  • Holographic d.o.f. (AdS/CFT, matrix models) — microstates live on a lower-dimensional boundary.
  • Entanglement bits (Van Raamsdonk's "spacetime from entanglement," HaPPY codes) — entanglement structure encodes geometry.
  • Strings and branes — strings as underlying objects, geometry as long-distance limit.

None is settled. All trace conceptual lineage to this 1995 paper. That's why Jacobson 1995 matters far beyond its length.
