The Frozen Star

Watch light crawl near a black-hole horizon — and watch Alice freeze and redden as she falls. The whole of Hawking's blueshift, ω_local ~ ω·e^{κu}, lives in this one picture.

The one fact driving everything: for an outgoing light ray in Schwarzschild, dr/dt = 1 − 2M/r. Far away that's ≈1 (light at full speed); right at the horizon (r → 2M) it's 0 — escaping light grinds to a crawl. Everything below emerges from that single line. Press play and watch.

② The exponential, made visible

Each dot below is one pulse arriving at Bob: its received frequency (vertical) against when it arrives (horizontal), on a logarithmic frequency axis. Exponential decay shows up as a straight line — that's the signature you're looking for. The slope is the surface gravity κ.

Alice emits her flashes at a steady rate by her own watch, yet they arrive at Bob ever-later and ever-redder, thinning toward silence. She never quite crosses — she freezes. (The climb-out time from height ε grows like −2M·ln ε: a logarithm. Invert it and ε ~ e^{−t/2M} — her height halves every fixed tick of Bob's clock.)

③ Now run it backward — the blueshift

Hawking doesn't watch Alice's light go out; he takes a wave Bob receives and traces it back toward the horizon. Same exponential, reversed: the deeper you look, the more blue it was. Drag the slider to place an emission point and watch the local wave bunch up — its frequency is ω_local = ω / √(1 − 2M/r), running to infinity at the horizon.

What you just discovered

Light crawls near the horizon → the climb-out time is a logarithm → invert it and everything is exponential: Alice freezes exponentially, her light reddens exponentially (out), and a wave traced inward blueshifts exponentially (in). The rate is the surface gravity κ — the very same κ that becomes the Hawking temperature T = κ/2π. Feed this exponential into a quantum field and the stretched modes come out thermal. That is Hawking radiation.
