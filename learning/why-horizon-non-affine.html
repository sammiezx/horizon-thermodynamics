Horizon thermodynamics · concept note

Why the Killing parameter is non-affine on a horizon

The real reason behind  $\xi^\nu\nabla_\nu\xi^\mu = \kappa\,\xi^\mu$  — and why $\kappa$ is the surface gravity.
The non-affinity is not a defect of the geodesics. The horizon generators are perfectly good null geodesics with a perfectly good affine parameter. The real question is: why does the Killing flow run along them non-affinely? The answer is one structural fact — the Killing vector generates a boost and must vanish at the bifurcation surface.

Step 0Clearing up what is being asked

On a null generator there is no proper time ($ds^2=0$), but the curve is still a geodesic, so it admits an affine parameter $\lambda$ with a parallel-transported tangent:

Affine $$k^\mu=\frac{dx^\mu}{d\lambda},\qquad k^\nu\nabla_\nu k^\mu = 0$$

i.e. $k$ is constant (parallel-transported) along the ray.

So an affine generator-vector is constant along the ray. The puzzle is that the Killing vector $\xi$ is not constant — it obeys $\xi^\nu\nabla_\nu\xi^\mu=\kappa\,\xi^\mu$. Why can't $\xi$ just be the constant affine tangent?

Step 1The one reason: $\xi$ must vanish at the bifurcation surface

This is the heart of it, so we go slowly. We will show $\xi=\kappa\lambda\,k$ in three small moves: (1a) $\xi$ can only be the affine tangent times some scalar; (1b) that scalar’s growth rate is $\kappa$; (1c) two physical facts force the scalar to be exactly $\kappa\lambda$.

1a — On a single generator, the only freedom is a scalar

Walk along one null generator. At every point the generator has exactly one null direction, and the affine tangent $k$ already points along it. So any vector that is tangent to the generator — including the Killing vector $\xi$ — can differ from $k$ only by a length. That is, there is some scalar function $h(\lambda)$ along the ray with

Setup $$\xi = h(\lambda)\,k .$$

$h$ is just “how long $\xi$ is, compared to the affine tangent, at each point of the ray.” Nothing has been assumed yet — this holds for any tangent vector field.

1b — The non-affinity $\kappa$ is exactly the growth rate of $h$

Now compute the non-affinity of $\xi=h\,k$ directly. Use $k^\nu\nabla_\nu k^\mu=0$ ($k$ is affine, hence constant) and $k^\nu\nabla_\nu h=\dfrac{dh}{d\lambda}=h'$ (contracting $k$ into a gradient is just $d/d\lambda$):

Compute $$\xi^\nu\nabla_\nu\xi^\mu = h\,k^\nu\nabla_\nu\!\big(h\,k^\mu\big) = h\big[\,\underbrace{(k^\nu\nabla_\nu h)}_{=\,h'}\,k^\mu + h\,\underbrace{k^\nu\nabla_\nu k^\mu}_{=\,0}\big] = h\,h'\,k^\mu .$$

Compare with the definition $\xi^\nu\nabla_\nu\xi^\mu=\kappa\,\xi^\mu=\kappa\,h\,k^\mu$. The $h\,k^\mu$ cancels, leaving simply $$\boxed{\ \kappa = h'(\lambda)=\dfrac{dh}{d\lambda}\ }.$$

Read this slowly

This is the whole secret. The surface gravity $\kappa$ is nothing but the rate at which $\xi$’s length grows as you move along the affine parameter. If $\xi$ kept a constant length ($h'=0$) it would be affine ($\kappa=0$). Non-affinity just means $\xi$ is getting longer (or shorter) as you slide along the ray.

1c — Two facts pin $h$ to a straight line through zero

So far $h$ is any function with $h'=\kappa$. Two physical inputs fix it completely:

  • Zeroth law of black-hole mechanics: $\kappa$ is constant along the horizon. With $h'=\kappa=\text{const}$, integrating gives $h(\lambda)=\kappa\lambda + c$ — a straight line.
  • Bifurcation surface: $\xi=0$ there, so $h(0)=0$, which kills the constant: $c=0$.
$$\boxed{\ \xi = \kappa\,\lambda\,k\ }\qquad\text{(starts at zero on $\mathcal B$, ramps up linearly along the ray)}$$

There it is, with every step justified: $\xi$ must grow linearly because its growth rate $\kappa$ is constant, and it must start from zero because it vanishes at the bifurcation surface.

Why $\kappa\ne0$ is forced

Suppose the Killing flow were affine, $\kappa=0$. Then $h'=0$, so $h$ is constant; combined with $h(0)=0$ that gives $h\equiv0$, i.e. $\xi\equiv0$ on the entire generator — no horizon at all. So a genuine (bifurcate) Killing horizon cannot be affine: $\kappa\ne0$ is unavoidable.
(The borderline case $\kappa=0$ is exactly the extremal black hole — zero temperature, and indeed it has no bifurcation surface; it sits infinitely far down the generator.)

Step 2See it in flat space — Rindler is the whole story

The boost Killing vector of Minkowski space, $ds^2=-dt^2+dx^2$:

Rindler $$\xi = a\,(x\,\pa_t + t\,\pa_x),\qquad \xi\cdot\xi = a^2(t^2-x^2).$$

$\xi\cdot\xi=0$ on the light cone $t=\pm x$ — that is the horizon. And $\xi=0$ at $x=t=0$ — the bifurcation point.

On the future generator $x=t$, the affine parameter is $\lambda=t$ and the affine tangent is $k=\pa_t+\pa_x$. Substitute $x=t$:

$$\xi = a\,t\,(\pa_t+\pa_x) = (a\,t)\,k = (a\lambda)\,k \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \xi^\nu\nabla_\nu\xi^\mu = a\,\xi^\mu,\quad \kappa=a.$$

Exactly the boxed form $\xi=\kappa\lambda k$. The Killing vector is the affine tangent scaled by the affine parameter itself — because it has to die at $\lambda=0$.

Step 3Same structure in Schwarzschild (Kruskal coordinates)

Schwarzschild’s time-translation $\xi=\pa_t$, written in Kruskal null coordinates $(U,V)$, is a boost in the $(U,V)$ plane:

Kruskal $$\xi=\pa_t=\kappa\,(V\,\pa_V - U\,\pa_U),\qquad \kappa=\frac{1}{4M}.$$

Vanishes at $U=V=0$ (the bifurcation sphere). On the future horizon $U=0$, the affine parameter is $\lambda=V$ and $k=\pa_V$, so $$\xi=\pa_t=\kappa\,V\,\pa_V=(\kappa\lambda)\,k.$$

Identical to Rindler. Hence $\xi^\nu\nabla_\nu\xi^\mu=\kappa\,\xi^\mu$ with $\kappa=1/4M$.

The answer in one breath

Essence

Geometric: the Killing vector must be zero at the bifurcation surface, so along each generator it grows linearly in the affine parameter, $\xi=\kappa\lambda k$, instead of staying constant. A linearly-growing tangent is non-affine, and $\kappa$ is its slope.

Group-theoretic: the horizon symmetry is a boost. A boost dilates its null rays, $\lambda\to e^{\kappa s}\lambda$, rather than translating them. Affine parameters are translated by their generator; the Killing generator scales them — and multiplicative scaling is $\xi^\nu\nabla_\nu\xi^\mu=\kappa\xi^\mu$.

Why this is the whole game

Because $\xi$ scales $\lambda$ multiplicatively, the affine parameter and the Killing time $u$ are related exponentially, $\lambda\propto e^{\kappa u}$. That single exponential is what turns the horizon-regular vacuum into a thermal bath for the distant observer, giving $T_{\text{Hawking}}=\kappa/2\pi=1/8\pi M$. The non-affinity, the exponential, and the Hawking temperature are the same fact — that the horizon’s symmetry is a boost with a fixed point.
