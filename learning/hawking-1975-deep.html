One of perhaps fifty papers from the last century that defines a field. The result — black holes emit thermal radiation at $T = \hbar c^3/(8\pi G M k_B)$ — is conceptually shocking (a horizon, classically a one-way membrane, is quantum-mechanically a thermal source) and computationally hard to follow in the original. Prologue · P1What it claims, in one paragraph

Quantize a free massless scalar field on the spacetime of a star that collapses to form a Schwarzschild black hole. Define the vacuum state $|0_{\rm in}\rangle$ on past null infinity $\Jm$ — empty space before the collapse. Compute the expectation value of the late-time particle number operator $N_\omega^{\rm out}$ measured at future null infinity $\Jp$. Result:

$$ \langle 0_{\rm in}|\,N_\omega^{\rm out}\,|0_{\rm in}\rangle \;=\; \frac{\Gamma_{\omega}}{e^{2\pi\omega/\kappa} - 1} $$

where $\kappa = 1/(4M)$ is the Schwarzschild surface gravity and $\Gamma_\omega$ is the classical absorption cross-section ("greybody factor"). The denominator is the Bose–Einstein distribution at temperature

$$ T_H \;=\; \frac{\kappa}{2\pi} \;=\; \frac{1}{8\pi M}\qquad\text{(in units $\hbar = c = k_B = G = 1$).} $$

The black hole radiates thermally even in vacuum. Three premises (free scalar QFT in curved spacetime, the equivalence principle, asymptotic flatness) and one calculation. The entire field follows.

Module 1Penrose diagram of a collapsing star

The relevant spacetime is not eternal Schwarzschild. It is a star (matter present) that at some time begins to collapse, eventually forming a horizon and a singularity. The Penrose diagram is:

Three regions matter:

  • Past null infinity $\Jm$. Where the "in" vacuum is defined — the field is in the standard Minkowski vacuum far before collapse.
  • Future null infinity $\Jp$. Where observers at late times collect the radiation.
  • The future horizon $\mathscr H^+$. Where modes that don't escape end up.

Module 2Coordinates $u$, $v$, $U$, $V$

Outside the star (Schwarzschild region), introduce the tortoise coordinate

$$ r^* \;=\; r + 2M \ln\!\left(\frac{r-2M}{2M}\right). $$

This stretches the horizon $r = 2M$ to $r^* \to -\infty$. Then define

$$ u \;=\; t - r^*,\qquad v \;=\; t + r^*. $$

These are the Eddington–Finkelstein "retarded" ($u$) and "advanced" ($v$) null coordinates. The Schwarzschild metric becomes

$$ \dd s^2 \;=\; -\left(1 - \frac{2M}{r}\right)\dd u\,\dd v + r^2\,\dd\Omega^2,\qquad r = r(u,v). $$
Definitions used throughout
  • $u \in (-\infty, \infty)$ labels outgoing null rays; $u = $ const lines are outgoing geodesics in the exterior.
  • $v \in (-\infty, \infty)$ labels ingoing null rays.
  • $\Jp$ is reached as $r \to \infty$ at fixed $u$ (i.e., $v \to \infty$).
  • $\Jm$ is reached as $r \to \infty$ at fixed $v$ (i.e., $u \to -\infty$).
  • The future horizon $\mathscr H^+$ is at $u \to \infty$ (i.e., $r \to 2M$ from outside).

The Kruskal coordinates $U, V$ are the affine versions. At the horizon, $u$ is a bad coordinate (its derivative w.r.t. proper distance vanishes); $U = -e^{-\kappa u}/\kappa$ is regular there, and is the affine parameter along null generators of the past horizon. Hawking does most of the work in $u, v$ — the $U, V$ pair only appears implicitly when he invokes the analytic continuation argument.

Worked — why $U = -e^{-\kappa u}/\kappa$ is affine

"Affine" means the parameter $\lambda$ satisfies the geodesic equation with no right-hand side: $k^\nu \nabla_\nu k^\mu = 0$ where $k = \dd x/\dd\lambda$. For a non-affine parameter $\sigma$, $k^\nu \nabla_\nu k^\mu = \alpha(\sigma) k^\mu$.

On a Killing horizon, the Killing parameter $u$ — i.e., the parameter that advances along the orbits of $\xi^\mu = \pa_u$ — is intrinsically non-affine. The definition of surface gravity is exactly this fact:

$$ \xi^\nu \nabla_\nu \xi^\mu \;=\; \kappa\,\xi^\mu. $$

The non-affinity is the surface gravity $\kappa$.

Rescale the tangent. Set $k = f(u)\,\xi$ along a horizon generator. Then

$$ k^\nu \nabla_\nu k^\mu \;=\; f\,\xi^\nu \nabla_\nu(f\,\xi^\mu) \;=\; f\bigl[f'(u)\,\xi^\mu + f\kappa\,\xi^\mu\bigr] \;=\; f\,\xi^\mu\,\bigl[f'(u) + \kappa f(u)\bigr]. $$

For $k$ to be affine, the bracket must vanish:

$$ f'(u) + \kappa\,f(u) = 0 \;\Longrightarrow\; f(u) = C\,e^{-\kappa u}. $$

The affine parameter. With $k = \pa_\lambda = f(u)\,\pa_u$, the chain rule gives $\dd\lambda/\dd u = 1/f(u) = (1/C)\,e^{\kappa u}$, so

$$ \lambda \;=\; \frac{1}{C\kappa}\,e^{\kappa u} + \text{const.} $$

Choose constants so $\lambda$ is regular at the horizon ($u \to \infty$):

$$ U \;=\; -\frac{1}{\kappa}\,e^{-\kappa u}. $$

This is the unique (up to scale) parameter that absorbs the surface-gravity non-affinity. Symmetrically, $V = (1/\kappa)\,e^{+\kappa v}$ is the affine parameter on the future horizon. Together $(U, V)$ form the Kruskal patch on which the metric is regular through the horizon — the form in which Hawking's analytic-continuation argument (Module 18) is cleanest.

Module 3Surface gravity $\kappa$

Definition The surface gravity $\kappa$ of a Killing horizon is defined by $$ \xi^\nu \nabla_\nu \xi^\mu \;=\; \kappa\, \xi^\mu\qquad\text{(on the horizon),} $$ where $\xi^\mu$ is the Killing vector that becomes null on the horizon. For Schwarzschild, $\xi = \partial_t$ and one computes $$ \boxed{\;\;\kappa \;=\; \frac{1}{4M}\;\;} $$ Equivalently $\kappa = \half f'(r)|_{r=2M}$ where $f(r) = 1 - 2M/r$.

$\kappa$ has units of inverse length (in $c=1$); it is the natural "blueshift rate" near the horizon. Hawking's result will give a temperature $T = \kappa/(2\pi)$ — the appearance of $\kappa$ is the geometric content that becomes thermal upon quantization.

Worked — compute $\kappa$ directly

With $\xi = \partial_t$, $\xi_\mu = (-f, 0, 0, 0)$. The Christoffel $\Gamma^r_{tt} = \half f\, f'$. So

$$ \xi^\nu \nabla_\nu \xi^r = \Gamma^r_{tt}\,\xi^t \xi^t = \half f f'(r). $$

On the horizon $f = 0$ and the formula looks degenerate, but the correct identification is via the limit. Use instead the equivalent definition $\kappa^2 = -\half(\nabla_\mu \xi_\nu)(\nabla^\mu \xi^\nu)\big|_{\rm hor}$ which gives $\kappa = \half f'(2M) = \half \cdot 1/(2M) = 1/(4M)$.

Module 3AWhy the horizon forces an affine parameter

Modules 2–3 produced the affine parameter $U = -e^{-\kappa u}/\kappa$ and the surface gravity $\kappa$ almost mechanically. But it is worth stopping on the question a careful reader asks right here: why must the horizon's null generators be parametrized affinely at all? The choice is not cosmetic — it is forced, for three escalating reasons, and the third one is Hawking's result.

Reason 1 — necessity

The horizon is a null hypersurface, ruled by null geodesic generators. Along any null curve $\dd s^2 = 0$, so proper time never advances — it is identically zero and cannot label points on the ray. We need some parameter, and the affine one is the unique canonical choice: it is the parameter for which the tangent is parallel-transported,

$$ \kk = \frac{\dd x^\mu}{\dd\lambda},\qquad k^\nu\nabla_\nu k^\mu = 0. $$

Only with an affine $\lambda$ do conserved quantities $\xi_\mu k^\mu = \text{const}$ hold along the ray and the Raychaudhuri focusing equation take its standard form. A non-affine parameter injects a spurious surface-gravity term, $k^\nu\nabla_\nu k^\mu = \alpha\,k^\mu$, into every one of these.

Reason 2 — regularity (the coordinate region)

Two parameters compete on the horizon. The Killing parameter $u$ (advancing along $\xi = \pa_u$) is non-affine, $\xi^\nu\nabla_\nu\xi^\mu = \kappa\,\xi^\mu$; the affine $U$ obeys $k^\nu\nabla_\nu k^\mu = 0$. They are related logarithmically:

$$ U = -\frac{1}{\kappa}\,e^{-\kappa u}\qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad u = -\frac{1}{\kappa}\ln(-\kappa U). $$

Now watch the horizon limit. As a ray approaches $\Jp$'s past horizon $\mathscr H^+$,

$$ U \to 0^-\ \text{(finite, smooth)},\qquad u \to +\infty\ \text{(diverges)}. $$

The Killing/Schwarzschild time never reaches the horizon — an infalling ray takes infinite $u$ to arrive, though it crosses at finite $U$ (and finite proper time). This is the $r\to 2M$ coordinate pathology of Module 2: $u$ simply does not cover the horizon. The affine parameter does. Kruskal $U,V$ are nothing but the affine parameters along the generators, which is exactly why Kruskal is the chart regular across $\mathscr H^\pm$. So affineness is not a convenience — it is the only parametrization whose domain includes the horizon at all.

Reason 3 — it manufactures the spectrum

The exponential map between $U$ and $u$ does the real work. An outgoing mode the external observer calls positive-frequency, $e^{-i\omega u}$, written in the affine parameter becomes a logarithm in $U$:

$$ e^{-i\omega u} = \exp\!\Big[\tfrac{i\omega}{\kappa}\ln(-\kappa U)\Big]. $$

A function purely positive-frequency in $u$ is not purely positive-frequency in $U$: Fourier-analyzing $\exp[(i\omega/\kappa)\ln(-\kappa U)]$ against the affine modes $e^{-i\omega' U}$ returns both signs, with their magnitudes locked in a fixed ratio (Modules 17–18):

$$ \left|\frac{\beta_{\omega\omega'}}{\alpha_{\omega\omega'}}\right| = e^{-\pi\omega/\kappa}\;\Longrightarrow\; |\beta_\omega|^2 = \frac{1}{e^{2\pi\omega/\kappa}-1}. $$

That is a Bose–Einstein distribution at $T = \kappa/2\pi$. The thermality is precisely the statement positive-frequency-in-affine-$U$ $\neq$ positive-frequency-in-Killing-$u$, with the mismatch fixed by the affine↔Killing exponential.

Null generator has no proper time $\;\Rightarrow\;$ use affine $U$ $\;\Rightarrow\;$ $U$ is the regular parameter that crosses the horizon while Killing $u$ diverges $\;\Rightarrow\;$ the log relation $u = -\kappa^{-1}\ln(-\kappa U)$ turns the horizon-regular vacuum into a thermal bath.
Where this lands in the paper

This is the conceptual content of the logarithm $u \simeq -\kappa^{-1}\ln(v_0 - v)$ derived in Module 13 and continued in Modules 17–18. When you hit that step in Hawking, the quantity playing the role of the smooth, horizon-crossing coordinate is the affine parameter; the retarded time $u$ is the non-affine Killing time. That single logarithm is the affine↔Killing relation, and it is the entire origin of the Planck factor. If the text states the log without flagging that distinction, this is the line to slow down on.

Module 4The scalar field action

Hawking computes for a free, massless, minimally coupled real scalar field $\phi(x)$:

$$ S \;=\; -\half \int \dd^4 x\,\sqrt{-g}\;g^{\mu\nu}\,\pa_\mu \phi\,\pa_\nu \phi. $$

Equation of motion:

$$ \Box \phi \;\equiv\; \frac{1}{\sqrt{-g}}\,\pa_\mu\!\left(\sqrt{-g}\,g^{\mu\nu}\pa_\nu \phi\right) \;=\; 0. $$

The choice of a scalar field is technical convenience. The result extends to higher spins (Module 26) with mode-specific modifications. The choice of massless is also for technical clarity; massive fields are treated similarly but with $\omega \ge m$.

Module 5Klein–Gordon inner product

For two solutions $\phi_1, \phi_2$ of the KG equation, the conserved inner product is

$$ (\phi_1, \phi_2) \;=\; -i \int_\Sigma \dd\Sigma^\mu\,\bigl(\phi_1\,\pa_\mu\phi_2^* - \phi_2^*\,\pa_\mu\phi_1\bigr) $$

where $\Sigma$ is a Cauchy surface (any complete spacelike hypersurface, or asymptotically a null surface like $\Jm$ or $\Jp \cup \mathscr H^+$). The product is independent of the choice of $\Sigma$ by Stokes/Gauss.

Specialization to a null surface On $\Jm$ (a future-pointing null surface) parameterized by $v$ at fixed angles, the volume element reduces to $dv\,d^2\Omega\, r^2$, and the inner product collapses to $$ (\phi_1, \phi_2)_{\Jm} \;=\; -i\int \dd v\,\dd^2\Omega\, r^2\,(\phi_1\,\pa_v\phi_2^* - \phi_2^*\,\pa_v\phi_1)\big|_{\Jm}. $$ Hawking absorbs the $r^2 d^2\Omega$ into the normalization of the mode functions $\phi \sim Y_{\ell m}(\Omega)/r$ at large $r$. Then only the $v$-integral remains.
Make this intuitive — why the $r^2$ and the angles just disappear

Each piece looks like machinery but is a one-line physical fact. The whole step is just: count the wave’s wiggles as they cross a giant sphere, and the geometry bookkeeping cancels.

1. Why we may move to $\Jm$ at all — conserved flux. The inner product is a conserved current: like the total water crossing a river, it gives the same number through any cross-section. So slide the cross-section out to where the waves are simplest. $\Jm$ (past null infinity — where the incoming waves come from) is just the most convenient cross-section.

2. Why $r^2$ cancels — the light-bulb argument. A spherical wave dims as it spreads: amplitude $\sim 1/r$, so intensity $\sim 1/r^2$. But the sphere it crosses has area $\sim r^2$. The total crossing the sphere is

$$\underbrace{\tfrac{1}{r^2}}_{\text{intensity}}\times\underbrace{r^2}_{\text{area}}=\text{constant}.$$

The dimming and the growing sphere cancel exactly — that is just energy conservation (a light bulb pushes the same total power through every sphere around it, however large). So the $r^2$ in the measure eats the $1/r$ from each of the two mode factors, and $r$ drops out: the answer does not care how big the sphere is.

3. Why the angles integrate to $1$ — drum modes. The spherical harmonics $Y_{\ell m}(\Omega)$ are the independent vibration patterns of a sphere — its “notes,” like a drumhead’s. Different patterns do not overlap (orthogonal $\to 0$); each is normalized ($\to 1$):

$$\int \dd^2\Omega\;\, Y_{\ell m}\,Y_{\ell'm'}^* = \delta_{\ell\ell'}\,\delta_{mm'}.$$

So summing over the sphere just asks “same pattern?” — yes gives $1$, no gives $0$. The labels $(\ell,m)$ never mix (spherical symmetry); they ride along as spectators.

4. Why $\pa_v$ — the only live direction. Sitting on $\Jm$ at a fixed angle and at $r\to\infty$, the one thing that changes as the wave sweeps past is the advanced time $v$ (the arrival time of the incoming ray). So the “time derivative” in the current has nowhere to point but along $v$.

Net effect

Geometry ($r^2$) cancels the spreading ($1/r^2$); the angles ($Y_{\ell m}$) integrate to a Kronecker delta; only $v$ survives. The 4-D integral becomes a 1-D count of the wave’s oscillations in $v$ — and with $\phi\sim e^{-i\omega v}$ that is an ordinary Fourier overlap, which is all the Bogoliubov coefficients (Modules 16–18) ever need. Same story on $\Jp$ with $u$ in place of $v$.

Spherical harmonics, properly — from the circle to orbitals to the $\delta$’s

Worth nailing once and for all — the same objects run through this calculation, the hydrogen atom, and the cosmic microwave background. Build them up from something you already know.

Start on a circle (the 1-D warm-up)

On a circle, any periodic function expands in Fourier modes $e^{im\phi}$, $m\in\mathbb{Z}$. Here $m$ counts how many wavelengths fit around the circle, and the modes are orthogonal:

$$\int_0^{2\pi} e^{im\phi}\,e^{-im'\phi}\,\dd\phi = 2\pi\,\delta_{mm'}.$$

Spherical harmonics are exactly this idea on the 2-sphere: standing waves, labelled by integers, orthogonal, and complete.

The patterns, and their two labels

A spherical harmonic factorises into a "round the equator" part and a "pole-to-pole" part:

$$Y_{\ell m}(\theta,\phi)=N_{\ell m}\,\underbrace{P_\ell^{m}(\cos\theta)}_{\text{pole}\,\to\,\text{pole}}\,\underbrace{e^{im\phi}}_{\text{around equator}},\qquad \ell=0,1,2,\dots,\quad m=-\ell,\dots,+\ell.$$

For each degree $\ell$ there are $2\ell+1$ patterns. You can read both labels straight off the picture:

  • $\ell$ = total number of nodal lines — how wiggly the pattern is overall, its "pitch." It is the eigenvalue label: $L^2\,Y_{\ell m}=\ell(\ell+1)\,Y_{\ell m}$.
  • $m$ = how much of that wiggle runs around the equator. Exactly $|m|$ of the nodal lines are meridians (longitude lines, from the $e^{im\phi}$); the other $\ell-|m|$ are latitude circles. $m$ also fixes the orientation / azimuthal twist.
The low ones — you have already seen these $$Y_{00}=\tfrac{1}{\sqrt{4\pi}}\qquad(\text{constant — the global average, a "monopole"})$$ $$Y_{10}=\sqrt{\tfrac{3}{4\pi}}\,\cos\theta,\qquad Y_{1,\pm1}=\mp\sqrt{\tfrac{3}{8\pi}}\,\sin\theta\,e^{\pm i\phi}\qquad(\text{dipole — 3 orientations})$$ $$Y_{20}=\sqrt{\tfrac{5}{16\pi}}\,(3\cos^2\theta-1)\qquad(\text{quadrupole})$$

The shapes $|Y_{\ell m}|^2$ are the atomic orbitals: $\ell=0$ the spherical $s$, $\ell=1$ the three $p$ dumbbells, $\ell=2$ the five $d$ cloverleaves. And the CMB power spectrum is just the sky’s temperature expanded in $Y_{\ell m}$ — each $\ell$ a finer angular scale. You have met these before.

Why "drum modes" is precise, not a metaphor

$Y_{\ell m}$ are the standing waves of the sphere — eigenfunctions of the spherical Laplacian:

$$\nabla^2_{\text{sphere}}\,Y_{\ell m}=-\ell(\ell+1)\,Y_{\ell m}.$$

Each carries a definite "frequency$^2$" $=\ell(\ell+1)$, its pitch. Solve any wave equation on a sphere and it rings as a superposition of these — exactly as a drumhead rings in its Bessel modes. This is also why they appear here: separating the wave equation on spherically-symmetric Schwarzschild forces the angular factor to be a $Y_{\ell m}$.

Orthonormality — the one property the inner product needs

Different patterns are "perpendicular" as functions (their overlap integral vanishes); each is normalised to one. With $\dd\Omega=\sin\theta\,\dd\theta\,\dd\phi$:

$$\boxed{\ \int Y_{\ell m}\,Y_{\ell'm'}^{*}\,\dd\Omega=\delta_{\ell\ell'}\,\delta_{mm'}\ }$$
Check it by hand

Normalised. $\displaystyle\int |Y_{00}|^2\,\dd\Omega=\frac{1}{4\pi}\int \dd\Omega=\frac{1}{4\pi}\,(4\pi)=1.$

Orthogonal. $\displaystyle\int Y_{00}\,Y_{10}\,\dd\Omega\ \propto\ \int\cos\theta\,\dd\Omega=2\pi\!\int_0^\pi\!\cos\theta\,\sin\theta\,\dd\theta=2\pi\Big[\tfrac12\sin^2\theta\Big]_0^\pi=0.$ A constant and a $\cos\theta$ simply average to zero over the sphere.

Completeness

Orthonormal and complete, so any function on the sphere is a sum of them — a Fourier series on the 2-sphere:

$$f(\theta,\phi)=\sum_{\ell=0}^{\infty}\sum_{m=-\ell}^{\ell} a_{\ell m}\,Y_{\ell m}(\theta,\phi),\qquad a_{\ell m}=\int f\,Y_{\ell m}^{*}\,\dd\Omega.$$
Why this nails the Hawking step

Schwarzschild is spherically symmetric, so the field separates, $\phi_{\omega\ell m}\sim \dfrac{R_{\omega\ell}(r)}{r}\,Y_{\ell m}(\Omega)\,e^{-i\omega t}$, and different $(\ell,m)$ never mix (angular momentum is conserved). In the inner product the angular integral is the orthonormality relation above, collapsing to $\delta_{\ell\ell'}\delta_{mm'}$ — every $(\ell,m)$ channel is independent. So Hawking solves one $\ell$ at a time; all $2\ell+1$ of its $m$-values give the same answer (degeneracy); and the angular harmonics ride along as spectators while the real work happens in the 1-D radial/time problem.

Module 6Positive/negative frequency and the vacuum ambiguity

To quantize, expand the field in modes:

$$ \hat\phi(x) \;=\; \sum_i \left( \hat a_i\, f_i(x) + \hat a_i^\dagger\, f_i^*(x) \right) $$

where $\{f_i\}$ are positive-frequency mode solutions, $(f_i, f_j) = \delta_{ij}$, $(f_i^*, f_j^*) = -\delta_{ij}$, $(f_i, f_j^*) = 0$. The vacuum is defined by $\hat a_i\,|0\rangle = 0$.

The crux of QFT in curved spacetime

The decomposition into positive and negative frequency requires a notion of "time," i.e., a timelike Killing vector. In a generally curved spacetime there is no such canonical choice. Different choices yield different mode bases and different vacua. The vacuum is not a property of the spacetime alone — it requires a choice of observer / Killing vector.

Two natural choices:

  • $|0_{\rm in}\rangle$ — defined by positive frequency w.r.t. $v$ on $\Jm$ (where the spacetime is asymptotically Minkowski, before collapse).
  • $|0_{\rm out}\rangle$ — defined by positive frequency w.r.t. $u$ on $\Jp$ (asymptotic late-time Minkowski region).

The Bogoliubov transformation between the two bases captures the particle creation. The whole result of Hawking 1975 lives in this mismatch.

Module 7"in" and "out" Hilbert spaces

The "in" modes $f^{\rm in}_{\omega'}$ are solutions positive-frequency w.r.t. $v$ on $\Jm$:

$$ f^{\rm in}_{\omega'}(v, \Omega, r) \;\xrightarrow{\;\Jm\;}\; \frac{1}{\sqrt{4\pi\omega'}}\,\frac{1}{r}\,Y_{\ell m}(\Omega)\,e^{-i\omega' v}. $$

Their creation/annihilation operators $\hat a^{\rm in}_{\omega'\ell m}$ define the in-vacuum $|0_{\rm in}\rangle$.

The "out" modes $f^{\rm out}_\omega$ are solutions of the KG equation that asymptote on $\Jp$ to

$$ f^{\rm out}_\omega(u, \Omega, r) \;\xrightarrow{\;\Jp\;}\; \frac{1}{\sqrt{4\pi\omega}}\,\frac{1}{r}\,Y_{\ell m}(\Omega)\,e^{-i\omega u} $$

and to zero on the future horizon $\mathscr H^+$. (There is a second family of out-modes localized on $\mathscr H^+$, but observers at $\Jp$ don't detect them; they are traced over.)

The relation:

$$ f^{\rm out}_\omega \;=\; \sum_{\omega'} \bigl(\alpha_{\omega\omega'}\,f^{\rm in}_{\omega'} + \beta_{\omega\omega'}\,(f^{\rm in}_{\omega'})^*\bigr) $$

defines the Bogoliubov coefficients $\alpha_{\omega\omega'}, \beta_{\omega\omega'}$. They are computed by the inner product:

$$ \alpha_{\omega\omega'} = (f^{\rm out}_\omega, f^{\rm in}_{\omega'}),\qquad \beta_{\omega\omega'} = -(f^{\rm out}_\omega, (f^{\rm in}_{\omega'})^*). $$

The number of "out" particles in the "in" vacuum is

$$ \langle 0_{\rm in}|\,\hat N_\omega^{\rm out}\,|0_{\rm in}\rangle \;=\; \sum_{\omega'} |\beta_{\omega\omega'}|^2. $$

The entire problem reduces to computing $|\beta|^2$.

Module 8In-modes on $\Jm$

Concrete form on $\Jm$:

$$ f^{\rm in}_{\omega'}\big|_{\Jm} \;=\; \frac{1}{\sqrt{4\pi\omega'}}\,e^{-i\omega' v}\,\frac{Y_{\ell m}(\Omega)}{r}. $$

$\omega' > 0$. The angular and radial structure is fixed; the time dependence is the plane-wave $e^{-i\omega'v}$. These are exactly the Minkowski plane waves in the asymptotically flat past — undisturbed by the (future) collapse.

Module 9Out-modes on $\Jp$

On $\Jp$ the out-modes are similarly clean:

$$ f^{\rm out}_{\omega}\big|_{\Jp} \;=\; \frac{1}{\sqrt{4\pi\omega}}\,e^{-i\omega u}\,\frac{Y_{\ell m}(\Omega)}{r}. $$

To compute the inner product $(f^{\rm out}_\omega, f^{\rm in}_{\omega'})$ on $\Jm$, we need $f^{\rm out}_\omega$ evaluated on $\Jm$. The out-mode is defined by its asymptotic behavior on $\Jp$; we propagate it back through the spacetime to $\Jm$.

Module 10Tracing the out-mode back through the collapse

The wave equation is hyperbolic; we can propagate the late-time data on $\Jp$ backward to $\Jm$ along null rays. The key insight, which Hawking states tersely and which deserves detail:

The geometric optics picture An outgoing null ray that reaches $\Jp$ at retarded time $u$ came from somewhere on $\Jm$. Trace it backwards:
  1. It propagates from $\Jp$ down to the star, entering the (then-collapsing) interior.
  2. Inside the star, it passes through (or reflects off) the matter — pick one boundary condition.
  3. It emerges as an outgoing ray during early times, before any horizon has formed.
  4. The "ingoing version" came in from $\Jm$ at some advanced time $v$.
The map $u \mapsto v$ is what we need to compute. Call it $v(u)$ or, inverting, $u(v)$.

For our purposes, the asymptotic relation at late $u$ (the relevant regime for observers far from the BH) is what matters. As $u \to \infty$, the ray spends ever more time hovering near $r = 2M$ before emerging; tracing it back through the collapsing star, it came in at $v \to v_0^-$ where $v_0$ is the advanced time at which the ingoing ray would have just reached the (forming) horizon. The Hawking effect is the logarithmic divergence of $u(v)$ as $v \to v_0^-$.

Module 11Why geometric optics is valid here

Hawking uses geometric optics — wavefronts travel along null geodesics, frequency is shifted by gravitational red/blueshift, no diffraction. Is this justified?

Near the horizon a late-time outgoing mode is blueshifted enormously when traced back. Its frequency at the matching point goes as

$$ \omega_{\rm local} \sim \omega\,e^{\kappa u} \;\;(\text{as } u \to \infty). $$

For any fixed $u$ large enough, $\omega_{\rm local}$ exceeds the spacetime curvature scale $\sim 1/M$. The mode is locally very high frequency compared to the geometry — exactly the regime where geometric optics is exact. This justifies the WKB approximation.

How $\omega_{\rm local}\sim\omega\,e^{\kappa u}$ — the derivation

This exponential is the same Kruskal relation from Module 3A, now wearing the hat of a blueshift. Two clocks tell the whole story.

The mode and the two clocks. The outgoing mode at $\Jp$ oscillates in retarded time $u$ — the distant static observer’s clock:

$$\phi\propto e^{-i\omega u},\qquad \text{phase}\ \psi=-\omega u.$$

That observer counts crests per unit $u$ and calls it $\omega=|\dd\psi/\dd u|$. A local observer near the horizon measures frequency against their own clock — proper time $\tau$, which near the horizon is the affine (Kruskal) parameter. So

$$\omega_{\rm local}=\Big|\frac{\dd\psi}{\dd\tau}\Big|=\omega\,\Big|\frac{\dd u}{\dd\tau}\Big|.$$

Everything sits in that conversion factor $\dd u/\dd\tau$.

The conversion factor is exponential. From Modules 2 and 3A, the regular (horizon-crossing) coordinate and retarded time are related by the Kruskal exponential, with $\tau\propto U$ near the horizon:

$$U=-\frac1\kappa e^{-\kappa u}\ \Longrightarrow\ \frac{\dd U}{\dd u}=e^{-\kappa u}=-\kappa U\ \Longrightarrow\ \Big|\frac{\dd u}{\dd U}\Big|=e^{\kappa u}.$$
$$\omega_{\rm local}\ \propto\ \omega\,\Big|\frac{\dd u}{\dd U}\Big|\ =\ \omega\,e^{\kappa u}.$$

As $u\to\infty$ — a ray emerging at very late retarded time grazed the horizon when traced back — $\omega_{\rm local}\to\infty$, the unbounded blueshift.

Why, physically

Distant time $u$ and the local near-horizon clock are related exponentially (the Kruskal map): one tick of the local clock is $e^{\kappa u}$ ticks of $u$, so the same wave train packs $e^{\kappa u}$ more oscillations per unit local time the closer you trace it. Equivalently it is plain gravitational blueshift — a static observer where the redshift factor is $V=\sqrt{1-2M/r}$ sees $\omega/V$, and the ray reaching infinity at late $u$ sat where $V\propto e^{-\kappa u}$, so $\omega_{\rm local}=\omega/V\propto\omega\,e^{\kappa u}$. Same $\kappa$, same exponential.

The same exponential, twice

This blueshift and the thermal spectrum (Modules 13–18) are the same fact — the affine↔Killing exponential $U\propto e^{-\kappa u}$. That is exactly why the trans-Planckian caveat below bites: the very mechanism that makes the spectrum thermal also drives $\omega_{\rm local}$ past the Planck scale.

But — the trans-Planckian problem (Module 28) As $u \to \infty$, $\omega_{\rm local}$ eventually exceeds the Planck scale, at which point unknown quantum-gravity physics presumably modifies the calculation. Hawking 1975 ignores this. Whether the final answer is robust against trans-Planckian modifications is a subtle, much-discussed question — the answer is broadly "yes" but requires careful argument (e.g., Unruh 1995 on the supersonic dumb-hole analog).

Module 12Setup of the matching

We now compute $u(v)$ as $v \to v_0^-$. The geometry is: a star with surface at $r = R(\tau)$ in some interior coordinates, exterior Schwarzschild for $r > R(\tau)$. A null ray comes in from $\Jm$ at advanced time $v$, propagates inward, reflects (or passes through) at the regular center, and emerges as outgoing — reaching $\Jp$ at retarded time $u$.

For $v$ close to $v_0$, the outgoing ray emerges very close to (but outside) the soon-to-be horizon. Such a ray takes a long time to "climb out" to large $r$. The exact form of the matching depends on the collapse details, but the asymptotic form $u(v)$ as $v \to v_0^-$ is universal — controlled only by $\kappa$.

Module 13Derivation of $u \approx -\kappa^{-1}\ln(v_0 - v)$

This is the central technical step. Hawking states it as eq. (2.4)–(2.6) of the original paper without full derivation. Here it is.

Step 1. Consider an outgoing null geodesic in the exterior Schwarzschild region, hovering just outside the horizon. Its affine parameter satisfies

$$ \frac{\dd r}{\dd u} \;=\; \half\,\bigl(1 - \tfrac{2M}{r}\bigr) \;\equiv\; \half\,f(r). $$

Near the horizon, $f(r) \approx \kappa\cdot 2(r - 2M) \cdot (1/(2M))$. More precisely:

$$ f(r) = \frac{r - 2M}{r} \approx \frac{r - 2M}{2M} \;\;\text{as } r \to 2M. $$

Let $\rho \equiv r - 2M$. Then $f \approx \rho/(2M)$ and $\dd r/\dd u = \dd\rho/\dd u \approx \rho/(4M) = \kappa\rho$. Solve:

$$ \rho(u) \approx \rho_0\,e^{\kappa u},\qquad r(u) - 2M \approx \rho_0\,e^{\kappa u}. $$

Step 2. The ingoing ray that, after reflection from the center, becomes this outgoing ray, hits the star's surface at some $r = r_s(v)$. The Killing-energy of the photon is conserved: $\omega_v = $ constant along the trajectory. The relationship between the ingoing $v$ and the outgoing $u$ comes from the geometry at the reflection point.

Step 3. Inside the collapsing star and at the reflection (center), the geometry is approximately Minkowski (or some smooth interior), and the matching is regular. The non-trivial part is the redshift between the outgoing-ray's behavior near the horizon and its asymptotic form at $\Jp$.

Step 4. The outgoing ray's local affine parameter $\lambda$ near the horizon is related to $u$ by $\lambda \propto e^{\kappa u}$ (since $u$ is the Killing parameter and $\lambda$ is affine, related by the Killing-vector norm $\propto e^{\kappa u}$ in this regime). Hence, $r - 2M \sim \lambda \sim e^{\kappa u}$ as $u \to \infty$.

Step 5. Tracing back to $\Jm$: the ingoing ray that, after reflection, emerges as the outgoing ray with retarded time $u$, came in at advanced time $v$ such that the geodesic matching across the star surface gives

$$ v_0 - v \;\propto\; r(u) - 2M \;\propto\; e^{-\kappa u}. $$

Inverting:

$$ u(v) \;\approx\; -\kappa^{-1}\,\ln\!\left(\frac{v_0 - v}{C}\right)\qquad\text{(as $v \to v_0^-$)} $$

where $C$ is a constant of order $M$ depending on the collapse details. This is the universal late-time form of the Hawking effect. The logarithm is geometry; $\kappa$ is the rate at which late-time outgoing rays "remember" earlier ingoing rays.

Module 14Why the logarithm is universal (and the matching detail doesn't matter)

Different choices of collapse — dust, fluid, scalar-field — give different constants $C$, $v_0$ in $u(v) = -\kappa^{-1}\ln((v_0-v)/C)$. But the functional form $-\kappa^{-1}\ln(\cdot)$ is fixed by the existence of the horizon and the value of $\kappa$ alone. This is what makes the Hawking effect universal: it depends only on the surface gravity, not on the matter content. (Hence "an equation of state" — the temperature comes from the boundary, not the bulk.)

Module 15The out-mode evaluated on $\Jm$

With $u(v) = -\kappa^{-1}\ln((v_0 - v)/C)$ for $v < v_0$, the out-mode $e^{-i\omega u}$ propagated back to $\Jm$ reads:

$$ f^{\rm out}_\omega\big|_{\Jm} \;=\; \frac{1}{\sqrt{4\pi\omega}}\,\exp\!\Bigl[-i\omega\cdot\Bigl(-\kappa^{-1}\ln\!\tfrac{v_0-v}{C}\Bigr)\Bigr] $$ $$ \;=\; \frac{1}{\sqrt{4\pi\omega}}\,C^{-i\omega/\kappa}\,(v_0 - v)^{i\omega/\kappa}\quad(v < v_0), $$

and zero for $v > v_0$ (those rays fell into the black hole). The angular factor is absorbed into the normalization.

Module 16The two integrals — $\alpha$ and $\beta$

Substitute into the KG inner product on $\Jm$:

$$ \alpha_{\omega\omega'} \;=\; -i\int_{-\infty}^{v_0}\dd v\,\bigl[f^{\rm out}_\omega\,\pa_v (f^{\rm in}_{\omega'})^* - (f^{\rm in}_{\omega'})^*\,\pa_v f^{\rm out}_\omega\bigr]. $$

With $f^{\rm in}_{\omega'} = e^{-i\omega' v}/\sqrt{4\pi\omega'}$, the action of $i\pa_v$ on $(f^{\rm in})^*$ pulls down $-\omega'$. After bookkeeping, the inner product reduces, in the relevant regime $v$ near $v_0$, to

$$ \alpha_{\omega\omega'} \;\propto\; \sqrt{\frac{\omega'}{\omega}}\,C^{-i\omega/\kappa}\,e^{-i\omega' v_0}\int_0^\infty x^{i\omega/\kappa}\,e^{-i\omega' x}\,\dd x, $$

$$ \beta_{\omega\omega'} \;\propto\; \sqrt{\frac{\omega'}{\omega}}\,C^{-i\omega/\kappa}\,e^{+i\omega' v_0}\int_0^\infty x^{i\omega/\kappa}\,e^{+i\omega' x}\,\dd x, $$

where the substitution $x = v_0 - v$ has been made and the lower limit of integration extended to $-\infty$ on $\Jm$ giving the upper limit $\infty$ in $x$. The minor numerical constants are absorbed; only the integrals matter for the ratio $|\beta|/|\alpha|$.

Module 17The integrals — analytic continuation

Both integrals are of the form $\int_0^\infty x^s\,e^{\pm i\omega' x}\,\dd x$ with $s = i\omega/\kappa$. They are not absolutely convergent at $x \to \infty$ but are conditionally convergent. The standard trick is to regularize with a small damping $e^{-\epsilon x}$ and take $\epsilon \to 0^+$ at the end.

Step 17.1 — the regularized integral

For $\epsilon > 0$ and $\omega' > 0$:

$$ \int_0^\infty x^s\,e^{-\epsilon x + i\omega' x}\,\dd x \;=\; \frac{\Gamma(s+1)}{(\epsilon - i\omega')^{s+1}}. $$

This is the standard Laplace-transform identity, valid for $\mathrm{Re}(s) > -1$.

Step 17.2 — taking $\epsilon \to 0^+$

The denominator $(\epsilon - i\omega')$ has positive real part for $\epsilon > 0$ and tends to $-i\omega'$ from the right half-plane. Its phase tends to $-\pi/2$ from above. So

$$ \lim_{\epsilon\to 0^+}(\epsilon - i\omega')^{s+1} \;=\; (\omega')^{s+1}\,e^{-i\pi(s+1)/2}. $$

Step 17.3 — explicit evaluation

Plug in $s = i\omega/\kappa$:

$$ e^{-i\pi(i\omega/\kappa + 1)/2} \;=\; e^{\pi\omega/(2\kappa)}\cdot e^{-i\pi/2} \;=\; -i\,e^{\pi\omega/(2\kappa)}. $$

So

$$ \int_0^\infty x^{i\omega/\kappa}\,e^{i\omega' x}\,\dd x \;=\; \frac{\Gamma(i\omega/\kappa + 1)}{(\omega')^{i\omega/\kappa + 1}}\,(-i)^{-1}\,e^{-\pi\omega/(2\kappa)} $$ $$ \;=\; i\,(\omega')^{-i\omega/\kappa - 1}\,\Gamma(i\omega/\kappa + 1)\,e^{-\pi\omega/(2\kappa)}. $$

Step 17.4 — the second integral

Replace $\omega' \to -\omega'$ in the formal sense. Equivalently, the denominator becomes $(\epsilon + i\omega')$, with phase $+\pi/2$. So

$$ \int_0^\infty x^{i\omega/\kappa}\,e^{-i\omega' x}\,\dd x \;=\; -i\,(\omega')^{-i\omega/\kappa - 1}\,\Gamma(i\omega/\kappa + 1)\,e^{+\pi\omega/(2\kappa)}. $$

Module 18The ratio $|\beta|^2/|\alpha|^2 = e^{-2\pi\omega/\kappa}$

Combining Modules 16 and 17, restoring overall constants:

$$ \alpha_{\omega\omega'} \;\propto\; \sqrt{\omega'/\omega}\,\Gamma(i\omega/\kappa + 1)\,(\omega')^{-i\omega/\kappa - 1}\,e^{+\pi\omega/(2\kappa)}\,e^{-i\omega' v_0} $$ $$ \beta_{\omega\omega'} \;\propto\; \sqrt{\omega'/\omega}\,\Gamma(i\omega/\kappa + 1)\,(\omega')^{-i\omega/\kappa - 1}\,e^{-\pi\omega/(2\kappa)}\,e^{+i\omega' v_0} $$

(up to identical numerical prefactors which cancel in the ratio).

Therefore

$$ \frac{|\beta_{\omega\omega'}|^2}{|\alpha_{\omega\omega'}|^2} \;=\; \frac{e^{-\pi\omega/\kappa}}{e^{+\pi\omega/\kappa}} \;=\; e^{-2\pi\omega/\kappa}. $$

This is the equation. Hawking 1975 eq. (2.18). Everything that follows is bookkeeping.

Where did $e^{-2\pi\omega/\kappa}$ come from, physically? The factor is the relative weight of positive and negative Minkowski-frequency components in the late-time out-mode. The out-mode, viewed on $\Jm$, looks like $(v_0-v)^{i\omega/\kappa}$ — a function with a branch point at $v = v_0$. The Fourier transform of a branch-point singularity gives unequal positive and negative-frequency weights; the ratio is precisely $e^{-2\pi\omega/\kappa}$ because rotating around the branch point by $2\pi$ in the complex plane multiplies the function by $e^{2\pi i \cdot i\omega/\kappa} = e^{-2\pi\omega/\kappa}$. This is the precise statement of "thermality from a horizon's analytic structure."

Module 19Normalization — $|\alpha|^2 - |\beta|^2 = 1$

The Bogoliubov coefficients also satisfy

$$ \sum_{\omega'} \bigl(|\alpha_{\omega\omega'}|^2 - |\beta_{\omega\omega'}|^2\bigr) = 1, $$

which follows from the canonical commutation relations for $\hat a^{\rm out}_\omega$ being equivalent to those for $\hat a^{\rm in}_{\omega'}$. Combined with $|\beta|^2 = e^{-2\pi\omega/\kappa}|\alpha|^2$:

$$ |\alpha|^2(1 - e^{-2\pi\omega/\kappa}) = 1\;\Rightarrow\;|\alpha|^2 = \frac{1}{1 - e^{-2\pi\omega/\kappa}},\quad |\beta|^2 = \frac{e^{-2\pi\omega/\kappa}}{1 - e^{-2\pi\omega/\kappa}}. $$

The last fraction simplifies:

$$ |\beta|^2 \;=\; \frac{1}{e^{2\pi\omega/\kappa} - 1}. $$

That is the Planck/Bose–Einstein distribution.

Module 20From $|\beta|^2$ to $\langle N_\omega\rangle$

The expected number of "out" particles of frequency $\omega$ in the "in" vacuum is

$$ \langle N_\omega^{\rm out}\rangle \;=\; \sum_{\omega'} |\beta_{\omega\omega'}|^2 \;=\; \frac{1}{e^{2\pi\omega/\kappa} - 1}. $$

(The frequency-by-frequency sum, with mode normalization absorbed, gives the per-mode result.)

Module 21This is the Bose–Einstein distribution

The familiar bosonic thermal expectation at temperature $T$:

$$ \langle N_\omega\rangle^{\rm thermal} \;=\; \frac{1}{e^{\hbar\omega/(k_B T)} - 1}. $$

Match exponents:

$$ \frac{\hbar\omega}{k_B T} \;=\; \frac{2\pi\omega}{\kappa} \;\Rightarrow\; T \;=\; \frac{\hbar\kappa}{2\pi k_B}. $$

Module 22The Hawking temperature

Substituting $\kappa = 1/(4M)$ (in $G = c = 1$) and restoring constants:

$$ \boxed{\;\;T_H \;=\; \frac{\hbar c^3}{8\pi G M k_B}\;\;} $$

For $M = M_\odot$: $T_H \approx 6\times 10^{-8}\,$K. Solar-mass black holes are much colder than the CMB — they absorb more than they emit. Evaporation dominates only for $M \lesssim 10^{11}\,$kg (primordial BHs).

Module 23The greybody factor

The spectrum is not a perfect blackbody. The radiation, generated at the horizon, must propagate outward through the curved exterior geometry — through an effective potential barrier (the angular-momentum barrier and the gravitational potential). Some fraction is reflected back into the black hole. The transmission probability through this barrier is the greybody factor $\Gamma_{\omega\ell}$.

Concretely, write the radial wave equation outside the BH:

$$ \Bigl[-\frac{\dd^2}{\dd r_*^2} + V_\ell(r)\Bigr] R_{\omega\ell}(r_*) = \omega^2 R_{\omega\ell}, $$ $$ V_\ell(r) \;=\; \bigl(1 - \tfrac{2M}{r}\bigr)\Bigl[\frac{\ell(\ell+1)}{r^2} + \frac{2M}{r^3}\Bigr]. $$

The greybody factor is the transmission coefficient of this 1D Schrödinger-like problem. The full result becomes

$$ \langle N_{\omega\ell m}^{\rm out}\rangle \;=\; \frac{\Gamma_{\omega\ell}}{e^{2\pi\omega/\kappa} - 1}. $$

This is the headline result, with the greybody factor restored. This is the formula that your centerpiece paper computes for various less-studied spacetimes.

Module 24Total power radiated

Integrate the energy flux over all modes:

$$ \frac{\dd M}{\dd t} \;=\; -\sum_{\ell, m}\int_0^\infty \frac{\dd\omega}{2\pi}\,\frac{\omega\,\Gamma_{\omega\ell}}{e^{2\pi\omega/\kappa} - 1}. $$

The result is approximately a Stefan–Boltzmann formula with corrections, $\dd M/\dd t \propto 1/M^2$. Page 1976 tabulated the precise coefficients for various particle species. The total luminosity of a Schwarzschild BH is

$$ L_H \;\approx\; 3.6 \times 10^{-8}\,\Bigl(\frac{M_\odot}{M}\Bigr)^2\,\text{erg/s.} $$

Module 25Rotation — superradiance

For a Kerr black hole (rotation $a$), the calculation generalizes. The result:

$$ \langle N_{\omega\ell m}\rangle \;=\; \frac{\Gamma_{\omega\ell m}}{e^{2\pi(\omega - m\Omega_H)/\kappa} - 1}, $$

where $\Omega_H = a/(r_+^2 + a^2)$ is the horizon's angular velocity. For modes with $\omega < m\Omega_H$ the exponent is negative — these modes are superradiantly amplified: they extract energy from the BH's rotation. Penrose process at the quantum level.

Module 26Charge and higher spins

  • Charged BH (Reissner–Nordström): shifted exponent involves the chemical potential for charge. Charged particles emitted preferentially.
  • Fermions: the denominator becomes $e^{2\pi\omega/\kappa} + 1$ (Fermi–Dirac), as expected for thermal radiation of fermions.
  • Higher-spin bosons: same Bose–Einstein form; different greybody factors $\Gamma$ depending on spin.

Module 27Black hole lifetime

$\dd M/\dd t \propto -1/M^2 \Rightarrow M^2 \dd M \propto -\dd t \Rightarrow M^3 \propto t_0 - t$. The BH evaporates in time

$$ \tau_{\rm evap} \;\sim\; \frac{M^3 G^2}{\hbar c^4} \;\sim\; 10^{67}\,\Bigl(\frac{M}{M_\odot}\Bigr)^3\,\text{years}. $$

Solar-mass BHs outlast the age of the universe by 57 orders of magnitude.

Module 28The trans-Planckian problem

The mode that emerges at $\Jp$ with frequency $\omega \sim T_H$ comes from a mode on $\Jm$ that was blueshifted by $e^{\kappa u}$ near the horizon. For BHs older than $\sim M$ in their own units (which is always, after a brief startup transient), the original frequency $\omega' = \omega\,e^{\kappa u}$ exceeds the Planck frequency. The calculation appears to require trusting QFT at energies beyond its known validity.

The robust resolution: replicate the calculation in an analog "dumb hole" (Unruh 1981) with a fluid acoustic horizon, where the high-frequency physics is regulated by lattice/atomic scales. The Hawking-like phonon spectrum survives. This is taken as strong evidence that the original answer is insensitive to the unknown UV completion.

Module 29Backreaction

Hawking 1975 treats the spacetime as fixed (test field on classical background). But the radiation carries energy away — the BH must shrink. Backreaction is ignored. The full problem (Hawking-radiation-with-backreaction) is a coupled, non-equilibrium, semiclassical-gravity problem; clean treatments require careful regularization of the stress tensor (e.g., Christensen–Fulling 1977) and are still studied. The Page curve discussion (your reading list Module 20.1.2) is exactly the controlled "what happens when backreaction is incorporated globally" question.

Module 30The information paradox — Hawking 1975 didn't quite see it

Hawking 1975 noted that the radiation is thermal — depends only on the BH's external parameters $(M, J, Q)$. The information about what fell in (which book, which star, in what state) seems lost. Hawking concluded that pure states can evolve to mixed states in the presence of horizons: a breakdown of unitarity.

This is the information paradox. The status as of 2026 — based on the islands literature you have on disk (Penington 2019, AEMM 2019, PSSY 2019, AMMZ 2019, AHMST 2020) — is that semiclassical gravity, properly treated including replica wormhole saddles, does reproduce a unitary Page curve. The microscopic mechanism is not yet fully understood, but the macroscopic question ("can the radiation be unitary?") is largely settled in the affirmative.
