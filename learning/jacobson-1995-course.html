Thermodynamics of Spacetime

A graduate mini-course built around T. Jacobson, "Thermodynamics of Spacetime: The Einstein Equation of State," Phys. Rev. Lett. 75, 1260 (1995), gr-qc/9504004.
7 lectures · worked examples · exercises with solutions · a qualifying-exam-level final problem set. Difficulty tags: standard hard research

Front matter0. Prerequisites & conventions

This course assumes: general relativity through the Einstein equation (Wald chs. 1–6), the canonical quantization of a free scalar field, and undergraduate statistical mechanics. The harder lectures (L6, L7) touch the research literature.

Conventions
  • Metric signature $(-,+,+,+)$.
  • Natural units $\hbar = c = k_B = 1$ unless restoring constants is instructive. Newton's constant $G$ kept explicit.
  • Greek indices $\mu,\nu,\dots \in \{0,1,2,3\}$. Riemann tensor $R^\rho{}_{\sigma\mu\nu} = \partial_\mu \Gamma^\rho_{\nu\sigma} - \dots$, Ricci $R_{\mu\nu} = R^\rho{}_{\mu\rho\nu}$.
  • Horizon generators are null vectors $k^\mu$; affine parameter $\lambda$.

Lecture 1Black hole thermodynamics

The historical and logical entry point. Before Jacobson can say "entropy of a horizon $= A/4G$," that statement has to come from somewhere. It comes from the laws of black hole mechanics and Hawking's calculation.

1.1 The four laws of black hole mechanics

For a stationary black hole with mass $M$, angular momentum $J$, charge $Q$, horizon area $A$, surface gravity $\kappa$, angular velocity $\Omega$, electric potential $\Phi$:

Four laws (Bardeen–Carter–Hawking 1973)
  • Zeroth: $\kappa$ is constant over the horizon of a stationary black hole.
  • First: $\dd M = \dfrac{\kappa}{8\pi G}\,\dd A + \Omega\,\dd J + \Phi\,\dd Q$.
  • Second: $\dd A \ge 0$ in any classical process (Hawking's area theorem).
  • Third: $\kappa \to 0$ cannot be achieved in finite steps.

The structural identity with ordinary thermodynamics ($\dd U = T\,\dd S - P\,\dd V + \dots$) is exact if $T \propto \kappa$ and $S \propto A$.

1.2 Surface gravity

Definition For a Killing horizon with null generator $\xi^\mu$ (a Killing vector, null on the horizon), the surface gravity $\kappa$ is defined by $$ \xi^\nu \nabla_\nu \xi^\mu = \kappa\, \xi^\mu \quad\text{(on the horizon)}. $$ Equivalently $\kappa^2 = -\half (\nabla_\mu \xi_\nu)(\nabla^\mu \xi^\nu)$ evaluated on the horizon. For Schwarzschild, $\kappa = 1/4GM$.

1.3 Bekenstein–Hawking entropy and the Hawking temperature

Hawking's 1975 calculation (derived properly in Lecture 2 via the Unruh effect) fixes the proportionality constants:

$$ T_H = \frac{\hbar \kappa}{2\pi},\qquad S_{\rm BH} = \frac{A}{4 G \hbar}. $$

These are the two inputs Jacobson will assume hold for any local horizon.

1.4 The Smarr formula and the first law

Worked example

Derive the first law from the Smarr formula. For Kerr–Newman, the Smarr formula (an integrated Euler relation, from $M$ being homogeneous of the right degrees in $(A^{1/2}, J, Q)$) reads

$$ M = \frac{\kappa A}{4\pi G} + 2\Omega J + \Phi Q. $$

Differentiate, using that $M = M(A,J,Q)$ is a homogeneous function and applying Euler's theorem. The scaling weights are $A \sim L^2$, $J \sim L^2$, $Q \sim L$, $M \sim L$. Euler's theorem for a function homogeneous of degree $\half$ in $(A, J, Q^2)$ gives back exactly

$$ \dd M = \frac{\kappa}{8\pi G}\,\dd A + \Omega\,\dd J + \Phi\,\dd Q, $$

the first law. The factor mismatch ($\kappa A/4\pi$ in Smarr vs $\kappa/8\pi$ in the first law) is exactly the homogeneity degree of $A$.

Exercise 1.1standard

Compute $\kappa = 1/4GM$ for Schwarzschild directly from the definition $\xi^\nu\nabla_\nu\xi^\mu = \kappa\xi^\mu$ with $\xi = \partial_t$.

Solution With $\xi = \partial_t$, $\xi_\mu = (-f, 0,0,0)$, $f = 1-2GM/r$. The only nonzero relevant Christoffel near the horizon is $\Gamma^r_{tt} = \half f f'$. Then $\xi^\nu\nabla_\nu\xi^r = \Gamma^r_{tt}\xi^t\xi^t = \half f f'$. The norm of $\xi$ vanishes at the horizon, and one finds $\kappa = \half f'(r_h) = \half \cdot \frac{2GM}{r_h^2} = \frac{GM}{(2GM)^2} = \frac{1}{4GM}$.

Exercise 1.2standard

A solar-mass black hole: compute $T_H$ in kelvin. Comment on why astrophysical black holes don't evaporate today.

Solution $T_H = \hbar c^3/(8\pi G M k_B) \approx 6\times10^{-8}\,(M_\odot/M)\,$K. For $M = M_\odot$, $T_H \sim 60$ nK — far below the 2.7 K CMB, so the hole absorbs more than it emits and grows. Evaporation dominates only for $M \lesssim 10^{11}$ kg.

Lecture 2The Unruh effect

The temperature in Jacobson's argument is the Unruh temperature of a local accelerated observer. This lecture derives it from scratch — it is the one genuinely quantum input.

2.1 Rindler coordinates

The right Rindler wedge ($x > |t|$) of Minkowski space is covered by $(\eta, \xi)$:

$$ t = \frac{1}{a}e^{a\xi}\sinh(a\eta),\qquad x = \frac{1}{a}e^{a\xi}\cosh(a\eta), $$ $$ \dd s^2 = e^{2a\xi}\bigl(-\dd\eta^2 + \dd\xi^2\bigr) + \dd y^2 + \dd z^2. $$

A worldline of constant $\xi$ is a uniformly accelerated observer; the one at $\xi=0$ has proper acceleration $a$. The boost Killing vector is $\chi = \partial_\eta = a(x\partial_t + t\partial_x)$, null on the horizon $x = \pm t$.

Worked example

Surface gravity of the Rindler horizon. With $\chi = \partial_\eta$, $\chi\cdot\chi = -e^{2a\xi}$. Using $\kappa^2 = -\half(\nabla_\mu\chi_\nu)(\nabla^\mu\chi^\nu)$ on the horizon, one finds $\kappa = a$ for the observer normalized at $\xi=0$. So the Unruh temperature $T = \kappa/2\pi = a/2\pi$.

2.2 The Unruh temperature via Bogoliubov coefficients

Quantize a massless scalar. Minkowski modes are positive-frequency w.r.t. $t$; Rindler modes positive-frequency w.r.t. $\eta$. They are related by a Bogoliubov transformation. Unruh's trick: positive Minkowski-frequency modes are bounded and analytic in the lower-half complex $u$-plane ($u = t-x$).

Worked example

The thermal spectrum. On the horizon a right-wedge Rindler mode behaves as $\phi^R_\omega \sim (-u)^{i\omega/a}$ and a left-wedge mode as $\phi^L_\omega \sim (u)^{i\omega/a}$. The combinations

$$ \Phi^{(1)}_\omega = \phi^R_\omega + e^{-\pi\omega/a}\,\phi^{L*}_\omega,\qquad \Phi^{(2)}_\omega = \phi^L_\omega + e^{-\pi\omega/a}\,\phi^{R*}_\omega $$

are analytic in the lower-half $u$-plane (continue $(-u)^{i\omega/a}$ through $u\to e^{-i\pi}|u|$, picking up $e^{\pi\omega/a}$), hence pure positive Minkowski frequency. The Bogoliubov coefficients therefore satisfy

$$ \frac{|\beta_\omega|}{|\alpha_\omega|} = e^{-\pi\omega/a}. $$

With the normalization $|\alpha_\omega|^2 - |\beta_\omega|^2 = 1$, the Rindler-particle number in the Minkowski vacuum is

$$ \langle 0_M|\, N^R_\omega\, |0_M\rangle = |\beta_\omega|^2 = \frac{1}{e^{2\pi\omega/a} - 1}. $$

A Bose–Einstein distribution at temperature $T = a/2\pi$. This is the Unruh effect.

Remark The same analytic-continuation structure ($e^{-\pi\omega/\kappa}$ from continuing a log-type phase around the horizon) is exactly Hawking's 1975 argument for an evaporating black hole. Unruh's contribution was recognizing that you need only the near-horizon region, not propagation to infinity — see Jacobson 2003 §6.2.1.
Exercise 2.1hard

Show that the Wightman function of a uniformly accelerated detector, $W(\tau) = \langle 0_M|\phi(x(\tau))\phi(x(0))|0_M\rangle$, satisfies the KMS condition $W(\tau) = W(-\tau - i\beta)$ with $\beta = 2\pi/a$, and explain why this is equivalent to thermality.

Solution sketch Along the accelerated worldline, the geodesic interval entering the Minkowski two-point function becomes $\propto \sinh^2(a\Delta\tau/2)$. The function $1/\sinh^2(a\tau/2)$ is periodic in imaginary time with period $2\pi/a$ and analytic in the strip $-\beta < \mathrm{Im}\,\tau < 0$. Periodicity in imaginary time with period $\beta$ is the KMS condition, which defines a thermal (Gibbs) state at temperature $1/\beta = a/2\pi$. The detailed-balance ratio of excitation to de-excitation rates is $e^{-\beta\omega}$.

Exercise 2.2standard

Restore $\hbar, c, k_B$ in $T = a/2\pi$ and compute the acceleration needed for $T = 1$ K.

Solution $T = \hbar a /(2\pi c k_B)$. Setting $T = 1$ K gives $a = 2\pi c k_B/\hbar \approx 2.5\times10^{20}\,\mathrm{m/s^2}$. Enormous — why the Unruh effect has never been directly observed.

Lecture 3Null congruences & the Raychaudhuri equation

The geometry side of Jacobson's argument: how the area of a horizon changes is governed by the focusing of null geodesics.

3.1 Congruences and the deviation tensor

Definition A congruence is a family of curves filling a region, one through each point. For an affinely parametrized null geodesic congruence with tangent $k^\mu$ ($k^\mu k_\mu = 0$, $k^\nu\nabla_\nu k^\mu = 0$), define the deviation tensor on the 2D transverse space: $$ B_{\mu\nu} = \nabla_\nu k_\mu. $$ Decompose into trace, symmetric-traceless, antisymmetric parts: $$ \theta = h^{\mu\nu}B_{\mu\nu}\ (\text{expansion}),\quad \sigma_{\mu\nu}\ (\text{shear}),\quad \omega_{\mu\nu}\ (\text{twist}), $$ where $h_{\mu\nu}$ is the transverse metric (the 2-metric orthogonal to $k$ and an auxiliary null $\ell$).

3.2 Deriving the Raychaudhuri equation

Theorem (null Raychaudhuri) $$ \frac{\dd\theta}{\dd\lambda} = -\half\theta^2 - \sigma_{\mu\nu}\sigma^{\mu\nu} + \omega_{\mu\nu}\omega^{\mu\nu} - R_{\mu\nu}k^\mu k^\nu. $$
Worked example — proof

Start from $k^\rho\nabla_\rho B_{\mu\nu} = k^\rho\nabla_\rho\nabla_\nu k_\mu$. Commute derivatives using the Riemann tensor:

$$ \nabla_\rho\nabla_\nu k_\mu = \nabla_\nu\nabla_\rho k_\mu + R_{\mu\sigma\rho\nu}... \;\Rightarrow\; k^\rho\nabla_\rho B_{\mu\nu} = -B_{\mu\rho}B^\rho{}_\nu - R_{\mu\rho\nu\sigma}k^\rho k^\sigma. $$

(The geodesic equation kills a term; the $B^2$ term comes from $\nabla_\nu(k^\rho\nabla_\rho k_\mu) = 0$ expanded.) Take the transverse trace $h^{\mu\nu}$. Using $h^{\mu\nu}B_{\mu\rho}B^\rho{}_\nu = \half\theta^2 + \sigma^2 - \omega^2$ and $h^{\mu\nu}R_{\mu\rho\nu\sigma}k^\rho k^\sigma = R_{\rho\sigma}k^\rho k^\sigma$ (the transverse trace of Riemann contracted with two $k$'s gives the Ricci contraction, since the $k k$ directions drop out), one obtains the stated equation.

Focusing theorem For hypersurface-orthogonal congruences (such as horizon generators), the twist vanishes: $\omega_{\mu\nu}=0$. With the null energy condition $R_{\mu\nu}k^\mu k^\nu \ge 0$, every term on the RHS is $\le 0$: gravity focuses light. This is the engine of the singularity theorems and, here, of the area–curvature link.

3.3 Area change to the order Jacobson needs

Worked example

At the bifurcation point $p$ of a local Rindler horizon, choose the congruence so that $\theta = \sigma = 0$ at $p$ (local equilibrium). Then $\theta, \sigma = O(\lambda)$, so $\theta^2, \sigma^2 = O(\lambda^2)$ are negligible at leading order, and $\omega = 0$ (hypersurface-orthogonal). Raychaudhuri reduces to

$$ \frac{\dd\theta}{\dd\lambda} \approx -R_{\mu\nu}k^\mu k^\nu \;\Rightarrow\; \theta(\lambda) \approx -R_{\mu\nu}k^\mu k^\nu\,\lambda. $$

Hence the area change of the horizon patch is

$$ \delta A = \int \theta\,\dd\lambda\,\dd A = -\int R_{\mu\nu}k^\mu k^\nu\,\lambda\,\dd\lambda\,\dd A. $$

Lecture 4Local Rindler horizons

4.1 The equivalence principle and normal coordinates

At any point $p$, choose Riemann normal coordinates: $g_{\mu\nu}(p) = \eta_{\mu\nu}$, $\partial_\rho g_{\mu\nu}(p) = 0$, with curvature appearing only at $O(x^2)$. So a neighborhood of $p$ is Minkowski up to curvature corrections — and we can import the entire Rindler construction of Lecture 2.

4.2 Construction

Definition (local Rindler horizon) Pick $p$ and a null direction $k^\mu$ there. The past horizon of the local boost about the spatial 2-plane through $p$ orthogonal to $k$ is the local Rindler horizon. $p$ lies on it; its generators are null geodesics with tangent $k^\mu$; the approximate boost Killing field $\chi^\mu = -\kappa\lambda\,k^\mu$ vanishes at $p$ (the bifurcation surface).

4.3 The approximate boost Killing field

The key subtlety In curved space, $\chi^\mu$ is not an exact Killing vector. In Riemann normal coordinates it satisfies Killing's equation $\nabla_{(\mu}\chi_{\nu)} = 0$ only up to $O(x^2)$ corrections (from the curvature in the metric). Because $\delta Q$ and $\delta S$ are computed to leading order in the size of the patch, these corrections do not affect the leading-order balance. This is the load-bearing approximation in Jacobson's argument — and the focus of much subsequent scrutiny (see L6).

4.4 Local equilibrium

"Local equilibrium" means choosing the horizon so $\theta = \sigma = 0$ at $p$ — the horizon is instantaneously stationary there. This is what lets us use $\delta Q = T\,\dd S$ (equality, reversible) rather than the Clausius inequality. When this fails (L6), one needs an entropy-production term.

Exercise 4.1hard

In Riemann normal coordinates about $p$, write the metric to $O(x^2)$: $g_{\mu\nu} = \eta_{\mu\nu} - \tfrac{1}{3}R_{\mu\alpha\nu\beta}x^\alpha x^\beta + \dots$. Show that the flat-space boost field $\chi^\mu$ fails Killing's equation only at $O(x^2)$, and estimate the size of the violation in terms of curvature.

Lecture 5The Einstein equation of state

Everything assembled. This is the content of Jacobson's paper.

5.1 Heat as boost energy

The heat flux across the horizon is the boost-energy flux of matter:

$$ \delta Q = \int_{\mathcal H} T_{\mu\nu}\,\chi^\mu\,\dd\Sigma^\nu = -\kappa\int T_{\mu\nu}k^\mu k^\nu\,\lambda\,\dd\lambda\,\dd A, $$

using $\chi^\mu = -\kappa\lambda k^\mu$ and $\dd\Sigma^\nu = k^\nu\dd\lambda\,\dd A$.

5.2 Entropy change

From Lecture 3, with $S = \eta A$, $\eta = 1/4G\hbar$:

$$ \delta S = \eta\,\delta A = -\eta\int R_{\mu\nu}k^\mu k^\nu\,\lambda\,\dd\lambda\,\dd A. $$

5.3 Impose the first law

Set $\delta Q = T\,\delta S$ with $T = \hbar\kappa/2\pi$:

$$ -\kappa\int T_{\mu\nu}k^\mu k^\nu\,\lambda\,\dd\lambda\,\dd A = \frac{\hbar\kappa}{2\pi}\cdot\Bigl(-\frac{1}{4G\hbar}\Bigr)\int R_{\mu\nu}k^\mu k^\nu\,\lambda\,\dd\lambda\,\dd A. $$

The $\kappa$, the signs, and the $\lambda\,\dd\lambda\,\dd A$ all cancel, leaving the pointwise statement

$$ T_{\mu\nu}k^\mu k^\nu = \frac{1}{8\pi G}\,R_{\mu\nu}k^\mu k^\nu \quad\text{for all null } k^\mu. $$

5.4 From null $k^\mu$ to a tensor equation

"For all null $k^\mu$" means $T_{\mu\nu} - \frac{1}{8\pi G}R_{\mu\nu}$ is proportional to $g_{\mu\nu}$ (the only symmetric tensor annihilated by all null contractions):

$$ T_{\mu\nu} = \frac{1}{8\pi G}\bigl(R_{\mu\nu} + f g_{\mu\nu}\bigr). $$

5.5 The cosmological constant as integration constant

Worked example

Impose local conservation $\nabla^\mu T_{\mu\nu} = 0$. With the contracted Bianchi identity $\nabla^\mu R_{\mu\nu} = \half\nabla_\nu R$:

$$ 0 = \nabla^\mu T_{\mu\nu} = \frac{1}{8\pi G}\Bigl(\half\nabla_\nu R + \nabla_\nu f\Bigr) \;\Rightarrow\; \nabla_\nu\Bigl(\half R + f\Bigr) = 0. $$

So $\half R + f = -\Lambda$ for a constant $\Lambda$, giving $f = -\half R - \Lambda$. Substitute:

$$ R_{\mu\nu} - \half R\,g_{\mu\nu} + \Lambda g_{\mu\nu} = 8\pi G\,T_{\mu\nu}. $$

Einstein's equation, with $\Lambda$ as an undetermined integration constant. The thermodynamics fixes the field equations but not the value of $\Lambda$.

Exercise 5.1hard

Carry the full derivation keeping the $O(\lambda^2)$ terms ($\theta^2$, $\sigma^2$) you dropped. Show they would contribute at higher order in the patch size, hence cannot modify the leading balance — but argue heuristically that they are the seed of the "entropy production" of Lecture 6.

Exercise 5.2standard

Why must the argument demand the first law for all null directions $k^\mu$ at all points $p$? What weaker equation would you get from a single null direction?

Solution A single $k^\mu$ gives only one scalar equation $T_{\mu\nu}k^\mu k^\nu \propto R_{\mu\nu}k^\mu k^\nu$. Requiring it for all null $k^\mu$ promotes this to a full tensor identity (mod $g_{\mu\nu}$); requiring it at all $p$ makes it a local field equation rather than a constraint at one event. Both quantifiers are essential.

Lecture 6Beyond equilibrium

Jacobson's 1995 argument assumed local equilibrium and recovered Einstein gravity. What happens for higher-curvature theories? This is where the program gets subtle — and interesting.

6.1 Wald entropy

Definition (Wald entropy) For a diffeomorphism-invariant theory with Lagrangian $\mathcal L(g, R_{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}, \dots)$, the black hole entropy is the Noether charge $$ S_{\rm Wald} = -2\pi\oint \frac{\partial \mathcal L}{\partial R_{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}}\,\epsilon_{\mu\nu}\epsilon_{\rho\sigma}\,\dd A. $$ For Einstein gravity this reduces to $A/4G$. For $f(R)$ gravity it gives $S = \frac{1}{4G}\oint f'(R)\,\dd A$.

6.2 $f(R)$ gravity and the failure of equilibrium

Eling–Guedens–Jacobson (2006)

If you assume $S = \frac{1}{4G}\oint f'(R)\,\dd A$ and demand the equilibrium relation $\delta Q = T\,\delta S$, you do not recover the $f(R)$ field equations. The naive balance is off by a term. To fix it you must allow an internal entropy production:

$$ \delta Q = T\,\delta S - T\,\dd_i S,\qquad \dd_i S \ge 0. $$

The field equations of $f(R)$ gravity emerge only with this non-equilibrium term.

Interpretation The extra term behaves like the entropy produced by a dissipative process — spacetime acquires a "bulk viscosity." So higher-curvature gravity is the non-equilibrium thermodynamics of spacetime, with Einstein gravity the special equilibrium (reversible) case. This both extends Jacobson's program and exposes its assumptions: equilibrium ⟺ Einstein.
Exercise 6.1research

For $f(R) = R + \alpha R^2$, compute $\delta S$ from the Wald entropy along the horizon generators and identify the term that cannot be written as $\delta Q/T$. Match it to the $\dd_i S$ of Eling–Guedens–Jacobson. (See gr-qc/0602001.)

Lecture 7The emergent-gravity program

Where Jacobson's result sits in the larger landscape, and where the open problems are.

7.1 Sakharov's induced gravity (1967)

The earliest "gravity is emergent" idea: the Einstein–Hilbert action arises as the one-loop effective action of quantum matter fields in a curved background, with $1/G$ proportional to a UV-cutoff-squared. Gravity as elasticity of the vacuum.

7.2 The holographic principle

't Hooft (1993), Susskind (1995): the degrees of freedom in a region scale as the boundary area, not volume — exactly the Bekenstein–Hawking scaling. AdS/CFT (Maldacena 1997) is the concrete realization: gravity in the bulk = a CFT on the boundary, with the bulk metric emergent from boundary data.

7.3 Entanglement and spacetime

Van Raamsdonk (2010): bulk geometric connectivity is built from boundary entanglement; cutting entanglement pinches off the geometry. Jacobson himself returned to this in 2015 ("Entanglement equilibrium and the Einstein equation," PRL 116, 201101): the Einstein equation follows from demanding that small causal-diamond vacua be in entanglement equilibrium — a quantum-information refinement of his 1995 thermodynamic argument.

7.4 Open problems

  • What are the microstates? (No agreed answer outside AdS/CFT.)
  • Does the argument fix the dynamics or only kinematic constraints?
  • The cosmological constant: thermodynamics leaves $\Lambda$ free; the observed tiny value is unexplained.
  • The approximate-Killing-field assumption: is it innocuous or load-bearing? (Active debate.)

P1standard

Derive the Euclidean periodicity of Schwarzschild and hence $T_H = 1/8\pi GM$. (Continue $t\to-i\tau$, expand near $r=2GM$, demand absence of a conical singularity.)

Solution Near $r = 2GM$, set $f \approx (r-2GM)/2GM$. Substituting $\rho = 2\sqrt{2GM(r-2GM)}$ turns the $(\tau,r)$ part into $\dd\rho^2 + \rho^2(\dd\tau/4GM)^2$. Regularity demands $\tau/4GM$ have period $2\pi$, i.e. $\beta = 8\pi GM$, so $T = 1/8\pi GM = \kappa/2\pi$.
