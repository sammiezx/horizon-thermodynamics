Silicon PIN-photodiode radiation & muon detector

Your own instrument: a reverse-biased BPW34 as a direct solid-state ionization detector, charge amp + discriminator + coincidence. Designed from the physics, not copied from a kit.

Section 0What this is — and an honest scope

A reverse-biased silicon photodiode is a real particle detector: a charged particle crossing its depletion layer frees electron–hole pairs, giving a charge pulse you amplify and count. Be clear-eyed about what comes easy and what takes patience:

Expectations

Radioactivity (alpha / beta / gamma) → easy and immediate. Big signals, lots of counts. You will detect natural and household sources the first evening.

Cosmic-ray muons → real but patient. A muon is minimum-ionizing, so its pulse is ~hundreds of times smaller than an alpha, and the diode area is tiny, so the rate is low. Muons are a coincidence experiment you run for hours, not seconds. The numbers in §2 and §4 show exactly why.

That split is not a defect — it is the physics, and it makes a great staged project (§6): get radiation working fast, then earn the muons.

Section 1How a diode senses a particle

Reverse-bias the BPW34. The depletion region becomes a sensitive volume with a strong internal field. A charged particle passing through ionizes the silicon; it costs

Key constant $$w_{\text{Si}} = 3.6\ \text{eV per electron–hole pair.}$$

So a particle depositing energy $E$ frees $N = E/w_{\text{Si}}$ pairs, i.e. a charge $Q = N\,e = (E/3.6\,\text{eV})\,e$. The field sweeps that charge out as a current pulse.

Two facts set the numbers. A minimum-ionizing particle (the muon) frees about 80 pairs per µm of silicon. Take the BPW34's biased depletion depth to be $d\approx 40\ \mu\text{m}$ (a realistic figure at ~20 V bias). An alpha instead stops and dumps its whole energy. Compare:

ParticleEnergy depositede–h pairsCharge $Q$
Cosmic-ray muon (MIP)~80 pairs/µm × 40 µm≈ 3 200≈ 0.5 fC
Beta (electron, ~MIP-ish)similar order, variable~few thousand~0.5–2 fC
Alpha (e.g. 5.5 MeV, stops)5.5 MeV (full)≈ 1.5 million≈ 240 fC
Read the table

The alpha pulse is ~500× larger than the muon's. That single ratio is why radioactivity is trivial and muons are hard — same detector, wildly different signal.

Section 2Signal vs noise — why coincidence is mandatory for muons

The front end is a charge-sensitive amplifier (CSA): an op-amp with a feedback capacitor $C_f$ that integrates the collected charge into a voltage step

CSA output $$V_{\text{out}} = \frac{Q}{C_f}.$$

With $C_f = 1\ \text{pF}$: a muon gives $0.5\,\text{fC}/1\,\text{pF} = 0.5\ \text{mV}$; an alpha gives $240\,\text{fC}/1\,\text{pF} = 240\ \text{mV}$. The muon step is tiny — hence a second gain stage, and a hard look at noise.

The noise floor (equivalent noise charge)

The dominant noise is the amplifier's voltage noise $e_n$ acting across the total input capacitance $C_{\text{in}}$ (detector + strays). Schematically,

Noise scaling $$\text{ENC} \;\propto\; \frac{e_n\,C_{\text{in}}}{\sqrt{\tau}},$$

where $\tau$ is the shaping time. Lower capacitance and lower-noise op-amps win. This is exactly why we bias the diode hard (shrinks $C_{\text{in}}$ from ~70 pF down toward ~25 pF and widens the depletion depth $d$, which also raises the muon signal).

With a TL072 (JFET input, $e_n\!\sim\!18\,\text{nV}/\sqrt{\text{Hz}}$), $C_{\text{in}}\!\sim\!30\text{–}45$ pF and µs shaping, the ENC lands around ~1000–2000 electrons rms. Put that next to the signals:

Signal-to-noise

Alpha (~1.5M e⁻) / noise (~1500 e⁻) ≈ 1000 → trivially detected.

Muon (~3200 e⁻) / noise (~1500 e⁻) ≈ 2 → marginal on a single channel. A threshold that rejects noise also clips many muons.

The fix

Stack two detector channels and demand a coincidence: a real muon punches through both within nanoseconds, while the two channels' noise is independent and almost never fires together. Coincidence converts an SNR-2 single channel into a clean muon tag. Upgrade path: a quieter front end (discrete JFET like 2SK170/BF862, or an AD743) pushes ENC down to a few hundred electrons and makes single-channel muons visible.

Section 3The circuit, stage by stage

  1. Bias. Reverse-bias the BPW34 (cathode to +18–24 V through a ~10 MΩ resistor; battery supply for low noise). Higher bias ⇒ thicker depletion (more signal) and lower capacitance (less noise).
  2. Charge amp (TL072). Diode anode → inverting input (virtual ground); feedback $C_f\approx1$ pF in parallel with $R_f\approx100$ MΩ–1 GΩ. Output pulse $=Q/C_f$, decaying with $\tau=R_fC_f$.
  3. Shaper / gain (second TL072). CR-RC band-pass, shaping time ~1–3 µs, gain ~×500. This both amplifies and optimizes SNR (cuts low-frequency drift and high-frequency noise). Muon now ≈ 0.25 V, alpha rails the stage.
  4. Discriminator (LM393). Compare against an adjustable threshold (pot) set just above the noise edge. Output = a clean digital "hit." Stretch it to ~1–10 µs (RC or monostable) so the coincidence has a window.
  5. Coincidence + DAQ (Arduino). AND the two stretched hits — in a 74HC08 or in firmware — count coincidences, timestamp, log. (§5.)
Two things that will bite you

Light. The BPW34 is a light sensor — any stray photon swamps it. The diodes must live in a fully opaque, metal (EMI-shielding) box. Grounding. fA–pA-level signals demand short leads, a star ground, and battery power; mains hum will dominate otherwise.

Section 4Rates & geometry — set your expectations

Sea-level muon flux through a horizontal area is roughly

Flux$$\Phi \approx 1\ \text{muon}\ \text{cm}^{-2}\,\text{min}^{-1}.$$

One BPW34 has an active area $A\approx 0.075\ \text{cm}^2$. So through a single diode:

$$R_1 \approx \Phi A \approx 0.075\ \text{min}^{-1}\ \Rightarrow\ \text{one muon every }\sim 13\ \text{min}.$$

Coincidence between two stacked layers cuts that further (geometric acceptance). The practical move is to wire several diodes in parallel per layer to grow the area:

Diodes / layerLayer areaRough muon coincidence rate (layers stacked close)
10.075 cm²~1 per 15–20 min
40.30 cm²~1 per few min
8–10~0.7 cm²~1 per minute-ish
Honest takeaway

Small silicon ⇒ low muon rate; plan overnight runs to build statistics. (This is precisely why big-volume scintillators win for muons — area is everything. Your edge here is that the diode build is fully yours and sourceable, and superb for radioactivity.)

Section 5Firmware — coincidence counter (Arduino)

Simplest robust scheme: stretch each channel's discriminator output to a fixed window, AND them in a 74HC08, and let the Arduino count the AND output on an interrupt. If you prefer software coincidence, capture both channels and check overlap:

// Software coincidence + rate logger. Channels on D2, D3 (interrupt-capable).
// Discriminator pulses assumed stretched to ~5 us.
volatile uint32_t tA = 0, tB = 0;        // micros() of last hit, each channel
volatile uint32_t coinc = 0;             // coincidence count
const uint32_t WIN = 5;                  // coincidence window, microseconds

void hitA(){ tA = micros(); if (tA - tB < WIN) coinc++; }
void hitB(){ tB = micros(); if (tB - tA < WIN) coinc++; }

void setup(){
  Serial.begin(115200);
  pinMode(2, INPUT); pinMode(3, INPUT);
  attachInterrupt(digitalPinToInterrupt(2), hitA, RISING);
  attachInterrupt(digitalPinToInterrupt(3), hitB, RISING);
}

void loop(){                              // print cumulative count + rate every 10 s
  static uint32_t t0 = 0, c0 = 0;
  if (millis() - t0 >= 10000){
    uint32_t c = coinc;
    float rate = (c - c0) / 10.0;         // coincidences per second
    Serial.print(millis()/1000); Serial.print(" s  total="); Serial.print(c);
    Serial.print("  rate="); Serial.print(rate, 4); Serial.println(" /s");
    t0 = millis(); c0 = c;
  }
}

Log the serial stream to CSV; the offline analysis (Poisson statistics of the rate, threshold scans, later the cos²θ angular fit) is pure software — your strong suit.

Safety

Voltages here are low (≤24 V) — safe. For radioactive test sources use only household-level items (thoriated welding rods, lantern mantles, smoke-detector Am-241, potassium-chloride salt); don't open sealed sources, keep them at arm's length, wash hands. All are weak and legal, but treat them with respect.
