u(v): the log map, and Kruskal coordinates

An ingoing ray (advanced time v) reflects through the centre and leaves as an outgoing ray (retarded time u). Drag v toward the last escaping ray v₀ and watch the outgoing ray climb out later and later — the relation is u = −κ⁻¹ ln(v₀−v).

Units: $2M=1$, $\kappa=1$, $v_0=1$ (so $u=-\ln(1-v)$ and the Kruskal coordinate is exactly $U=v-1$). The shapes are the real functional forms; the ray paths are schematic.

② The map u(v)

A finite window of $v$ just below $v_0$ stretches over all late $u$ — the late-time pile-up that becomes the radiation.

③ Kruskal U in action

Equal steps of Killing time $u=0,1,2,\dots$ land at $U=-e^{-u}$ — bunching exponentially toward the horizon $U=0$. That is why $U$ (not $u$) is the coordinate that stays smooth there: all the late-time rays spread over infinite $u$ live in a tiny regular interval of $U$.

What this shows

The whole of Module 13 in one move: rays a hair inside $v_0$ skim the forming horizon and so emerge exponentially later. The map $u=-\kappa^{-1}\ln(v_0-v)$ is just the near-horizon exponential $U=-\tfrac1\kappa e^{-\kappa u}$ (dramatic) composed with the smooth, linear reflection through the centre, $U\propto v-v_0$ (boring). Read it backwards and it is the frozen star; quantize it and it is the thermal spectrum.
