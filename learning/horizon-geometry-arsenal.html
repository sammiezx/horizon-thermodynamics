Mathematical Arsenal for Horizon Geometry

The ten pieces of machinery behind “the Killing vector is non-affine on the horizon” — built from scratch, each with a hand-computable example.
How to use this. Read top to bottom once. Every module ends with a worked example you can verify with pen and paper — actually do them. The single most important new word is generator (Module 6); everything before it exists to make that module land.

Module 1Curves and their parameters

Everything starts with a curve: a smooth map from a real parameter into the spacetime. You feed in a number, it gives you a point.

Definition

A curve is a map $\gamma:\R\to\mathcal M$, written in coordinates as $\lambda \mapsto x^\mu(\lambda)$. The number $\lambda$ is the parameter; the set of points it sweeps out is the image.

A crucial distinction you will use constantly: the image (the geometric track) and the parametrization (how fast you run along it) are different things. The same track can be run many ways.

Example

The unit circle, run two ways:

$$\gamma_1:\ (\cos\lambda,\ \sin\lambda)\qquad\qquad \gamma_2:\ (\cos\lambda^2,\ \sin\lambda^2).$$

Same image (the circle), different parametrizations. $\gamma_1$ runs at constant rate; $\gamma_2$ accelerates. Reparametrizing means switching $\lambda\to\sigma(\lambda)$ — relabelling points without moving the track.

Module 2Tangent vectors are derivatives

The single most important mental shift in differential geometry: a vector is a derivative operator, not a little arrow floating in an ambient space. The tangent to a curve is the rate of change along it.

Definition

The tangent vector to $\gamma$ is the operator $\dfrac{d}{d\lambda}$. Acting on any function $f$, the chain rule gives

$$\frac{d}{d\lambda}f = \frac{dx^\mu}{d\lambda}\,\frac{\pa f}{\pa x^\mu} \;\equiv\; V^\mu\,\pa_\mu f,\qquad V^\mu=\frac{dx^\mu}{d\lambda}.$$

So the tangent vector is $V=V^\mu\pa_\mu$: components $V^\mu=dx^\mu/d\lambda$, basis vectors $\pa_\mu=\pa/\pa x^\mu$. Vectors at a point $p$ live in the tangent space $\Tp$.

This resolves an old confusion

This is exactly why $\pa_\phi$ “is” a vector: the basis vector pointing along $\phi$ literally is the operator $\pa/\pa\phi$. There is no separate arrow. ($\pa_\phi$ and $\pa/\pa\phi$ are the same symbol; their dual partner is the one-form $d\phi$.)

Example

For the circle $\gamma_1=(\cos\lambda,\sin\lambda)$:

$$V=\frac{dx}{d\lambda}\pa_x+\frac{dy}{d\lambda}\pa_y=-\sin\lambda\,\pa_x+\cos\lambda\,\pa_y.$$

At $\lambda=0$ this is $V=\pa_y$ — pointing straight “up,” tangent to the circle at $(1,0)$. Exactly the arrow you'd draw, but obtained as a derivative.

Module 3Vector fields, flows, and integral curves

A single vector lives at one point. A vector field puts one at every point. Then you can ask: what curves “follow the arrows”? Those are the field's integral curves, and the whole family is its flow. This is the machinery behind both “generator” and “Killing flow.”

Definition

A vector field $V^\mu(x)$ assigns a vector to each point. Its integral curves are the curves whose tangent equals the field everywhere:

$$\frac{dx^\mu}{d\lambda}=V^\mu\big(x(\lambda)\big).$$

Threading one integral curve through every point gives the flow of $V$: advancing the parameter $\lambda$ slides every point along its own integral curve.

Example A — rotation

$V=\pa_\phi=-y\,\pa_x+x\,\pa_y$ on the plane. Solve $\dot x=-y,\ \dot y=x$: the integral curves are circles, and the flow is rigid rotation about the origin.

Example B — dilation (watch the fixed point!)

$V=x\,\pa_x+y\,\pa_y$. Solve $\dot x=x,\ \dot y=y$: $x=x_0e^\lambda$, so integral curves are rays from the origin, traversed exponentially. At the origin $V=0$ — a fixed point: the flow holds it still.

Hold on to two features here — exponential motion along the curve, and a fixed point where the field vanishes. The horizon's Killing field will do exactly this.

Module 4The metric and causal character

The metric measures lengths and angles, and in a Lorentzian spacetime it sorts vectors into three causal classes.

Definition

The metric $g_{\mu\nu}$ gives the line element and the inner product:

$$ds^2=g_{\mu\nu}\,dx^\mu dx^\nu,\qquad V\cdot W=g_{\mu\nu}V^\mu W^\nu.$$

A vector $V$ is  timelike if $V\cdot V<0$,  null if $V\cdot V=0$,  spacelike if $V\cdot V>0$  (sign convention $-{+}{+}{+}$).

Example

Minkowski, $\eta=\mathrm{diag}(-1,1,1,1)$:

  • $u=(1,0,0,0)$: $u\cdot u=-1$  → timelike (a worldline direction).
  • $s=(0,1,0,0)$: $s\cdot s=+1$  → spacelike.
  • $k=(1,1,0,0)$: $k\cdot k=-1+1=0$  → null (a light ray).

Null vectors are the knife-edge between the two — the directions light travels.

The strange fact about null vectors

A null vector is orthogonal to itself: $k\cdot k=0$ literally says $k\perp k$. This odd property is the seed of everything weird about null surfaces in Module 5.

Module 5Hypersurfaces and their normals

A hypersurface is a slice of one dimension less than spacetime — a 3D surface in 4D. The cleanest way to specify one is as a level set.

Definition

A hypersurface is $\Phi(x)=\text{const}$ for some function $\Phi$. Its normal one-form is the gradient $n_\mu=\pa_\mu\Phi$, and the normal vector is $n^\mu=g^{\mu\nu}n_\nu$. The surface inherits the causal character of its normal.

The null case — this is the whole point

If the normal is null ($n\cdot n=0$), the surface is a null hypersurface. Because a null vector is orthogonal to itself, the normal $n$ also lies tangent to the surface — it is simultaneously “sticking out of” and “running along” the surface. No spacelike or timelike surface does this. It is special to null surfaces, and it is what makes Module 6 possible.

Example

Minkowski, take $\Phi=t-x$, so the surface is $t-x=\text{const}$. Normal one-form $n_\mu=\pa_\mu\Phi=(1,-1,0,0)$. Its norm:

$$n\cdot n=\eta^{\mu\nu}n_\mu n_\nu=\eta^{tt}(1)^2+\eta^{xx}(-1)^2=-1+1=0.$$

Null surface. The normal vector is $n^\mu=\eta^{\mu\nu}n_\nu=(-1,-1,0,0)\propto(1,1,0,0)$. Check it lies in the surface: moving along $(1,1,0,0)$ changes $t-x$ by $1-1=0$ — yes, it stays on the surface. The normal runs along the surface, as promised.

Module 6Generators of a null surface (the key word)

Here is the word that was tripping you up. A null hypersurface is not a featureless slab — it is woven out of null rays, and those rays are called its generators.

Intuition first

Think of a cylinder in ordinary 3D space. Through every point of the cylinder runs a vertical straight line lying entirely on the cylinder. Those lines “weave” the surface — they generate it. A null hypersurface is the same idea, with null geodesics in place of straight lines.

Definition / Theorem

Every null hypersurface is ruled by a family of null geodesics called its generators. Through each point of the surface passes exactly one generator, lying entirely within the surface, and the null normal $n$ (from Module 5) is tangent to it. The surface is the union of all its generators.

This works precisely because of the strange fact in Module 5: the null normal is tangent to the surface, so “the direction sticking out” is also “a direction to travel,” and travelling along it traces a curve in the surface — a generator.

Example — the Rindler horizon

The null surface $t-x=0$ (with $x>0$) from Module 5 is woven from the null line in the direction $(1,1,0,0)$. Pick the generator through $(t,x)=(1,1)$: it is $\{(s,s):s>0\}$, a null ray. Sliding along it by the null normal $n\propto(1,1)$ keeps you on the surface. That ray is a generator of the Rindler horizon — and in Module 9 the boost Killing field will turn out to run right along it.

Translation

So whenever the note says “horizon generator,” read: “one of the null geodesics that the horizon is built from.” “Along a generator” means “moving along one of those null rays, staying on the horizon.”

Module 7Covariant derivative and parallel transport

To compare vectors at different points of a curved space you need a connection — the covariant derivative $\nabla$. Plain $\pa_\mu$ fails because the basis vectors themselves twist from point to point; $\nabla$ corrects for that with the Christoffel symbols.

Definition $$\nabla_\mu V^\nu=\pa_\mu V^\nu+\Gamma^\nu_{\mu\lambda}V^\lambda.$$

The directional derivative along a curve with tangent $k^\mu$ is $k^\mu\nabla_\mu$. A vector $V$ is parallel-transported along the curve if it doesn't change in the connection's eyes:

$$k^\mu\nabla_\mu V^\nu=0\qquad(\text{“}V\text{ stays constant along the curve”}).$$
Example — flat vs curved

Flat plane (Cartesian, $\Gamma=0$): parallel transport just keeps the components fixed — an arrow slid around stays pointing the same way. Sphere: carry a vector around a closed triangle and it comes back rotated by the enclosed area. That rotation (holonomy) is curvature, and it is why “constant” needs the connection, not plain $\pa$.

Module 8Geodesics: affine vs non-affine

A geodesic is the straightest possible curve: it parallel-transports its own tangent.

Definition

$\gamma$ is a geodesic if its tangent $k$ satisfies, for some function $\kappa$,

$$k^\nu\nabla_\nu k^\mu=\kappa\,k^\mu.$$

If $\kappa=0$ the parameter is affine and the tangent is genuinely parallel-transported ($k^\nu\nabla_\nu k^\mu=0$). If $\kappa\ne0$ the same geometric curve is run non-affinely — the tangent stays parallel to itself but rescales in length.

Example

Flat space, the straight line $x^\mu(\lambda)=A^\mu\lambda+B^\mu$ has $k^\mu=A^\mu$ constant, so $k^\nu\nabla_\nu k^\mu=0$ — affine. Re-run the same line as $x^\mu(\sigma)=A^\mu\sigma^3+B^\mu$: now the tangent grows, $k^\nu\nabla_\nu k^\mu\propto k^\mu$ with $\kappa\ne0$ — non-affine, even though the track is identical. Affineness is about the parametrization, not the track.

The hook into horizons

That $\kappa$ — the failure of a parametrization to be affine — is, on a horizon, the surface gravity. The whole non-affinity note is about why the Killing parametrization of the horizon generators has $\kappa\ne0$.

Module 9Killing vectors (symmetries)

A Killing vector is the generator of a symmetry of the metric — a flow (Module 3) that slides the spacetime onto itself without distortion.

Definition

$\xi$ is a Killing vector if its flow preserves the metric, equivalently if it solves the Killing equation

$$\nabla_\mu\xi_\nu+\nabla_\nu\xi_\mu=0.$$

Shortcut: if the metric components don't depend on a coordinate $x^a$, then $\pa_a$ is automatically Killing. Each Killing vector gives a conserved quantity $\xi_\mu k^\mu=\text{const}$ along any geodesic.

Example — the boost (remember Module 3B)

In Minkowski, the boost $\xi=a(x\,\pa_t+t\,\pa_x)$ is a Killing vector. Its flow is the family of accelerated (hyperbolic) observers; its integral curves are the hyperbolae $x^2-t^2=\text{const}$. Two features, exactly as in the dilation example:

  • It vanishes at the origin $x=t=0$ — a fixed point.
  • Its norm $\xi\cdot\xi=a^2(t^2-x^2)$ is zero on $t=\pm x$ — it goes null on a null surface (the Rindler horizon of Module 6).

Module 10Assembling the horizon picture

Now stack the pieces. Each phrase in the non-affinity note maps to a module:

Definition

A Killing horizon is a null hypersurface (M5) whose generators (M6) are orbits of a Killing vector $\xi$ (M9), on which $\xi$ is null (M4).

On such a surface, two descriptions of the very same null ray collide:

  • It is a null geodesic generator (M6, M8) — so it carries an affine parameter $\lambda$ with tangent $k$, $k^\nu\nabla_\nu k=0$.
  • It is also an orbit of the Killing flow (M9) — so $\xi$ is another tangent to the same ray.

Two tangents to one curve must be proportional (M6: only one null direction), so $\xi=h(\lambda)\,k$ for some length $h$. The single question left — is the Killing parametrization affine? — is whether $h$ is constant. It is not, because (M9 example) $\xi$ must vanish at the fixed point / bifurcation surface, forcing $h$ to ramp linearly: $\xi=\kappa\lambda\,k$. That ramp rate $\kappa$ is the surface gravity.
