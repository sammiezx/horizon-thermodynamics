§J1.1 — Einstein equation as thermodynamics (Jacobson §1, p. 4)

"The fact[1] that the semi-classical Einstein equation can be derived by demanding that the first law of thermodynamics hold for local causal horizons, assuming the proportionality of entropy and area, leads one to suspect that the metric is only meaningful in the thermodynamic limit of something else."

Jacobson 2003, §1 Introduction, p. 4

This is one sentence. It is also one of the most consequential off-hand remarks in modern theoretical physics. Reference [1] is to Jacobson's own 1995 paper "Thermodynamics of spacetime: The Einstein equation of state" (Phys. Rev. Lett. 75, 1260, gr-qc/9504004), and what it announces is sufficiently strange that the rest of the sentence — about the metric being a thermodynamic-limit object — is a cautious philosophical inference Jacobson is willing to defend in print. Walk slowly.

Context Jacobson 1995 derives the Einstein equation from four ingredients, none of which is GR itself:
  • Local causal horizons. At any point $p$ in any Lorentzian spacetime, you can pick a small patch of a null hypersurface near $p$ and treat it as the past horizon of a uniformly accelerated observer threading $p$ — a "local Rindler horizon." You don't need a global event horizon. Any spacetime point has light cones, so this construction is always available.
  • The Unruh effect. A uniformly accelerated observer with proper acceleration $a$ perceives the Minkowski vacuum as a thermal bath at temperature $T = \hbar a / (2\pi k_B c)$. This is pure flat-space QFT (Unruh 1976). For a local Rindler horizon with local surface gravity $\kappa$, this becomes $T = \hbar\kappa/(2\pi k_B)$.
  • Entropy proportional to area. Assume any local horizon carries entropy $S = \eta A$ where $\eta = 1/(4 G\hbar)$ in natural units. This is the Bekenstein–Hawking value, taken as input. This is the only place GR-specific information enters the argument — and even this enters as an assumption, not a derivation.
  • The first law: $\delta Q = T\, \delta S$. Heat flow across the horizon = temperature × entropy change.
Translation Plug these four ingredients in and demand the first law holds for every local horizon at every point. That single demand forces a tensor equation on the spacetime — and the equation that pops out is, exactly, $$R_{\mu\nu} - \tfrac{1}{2} R\, g_{\mu\nu} + \Lambda\, g_{\mu\nu} \;=\; \frac{8\pi G}{c^4}\, T_{\mu\nu}\,.$$ The Einstein equation. With a matter source. With a cosmological constant (which appears as an integration constant). Derived from thermodynamics, Unruh, and entropy-equals-area. Not assumed.
Skipped step The technical core of Jacobson 1995, in five lines:
  1. The heat flux through a patch of the local horizon, with null generator $k^\mu$ and affine parameter $\lambda$, is the energy flux of matter through it: $\delta Q = \int T_{\mu\nu}\, k^\mu k^\nu\, \lambda\, d\lambda\, dA$ (the extra $\lambda$ comes from the boost weight — the Killing energy crossing the horizon).
  2. The area change of the local horizon is given by integrating the Raychaudhuri equation for null congruences. To leading order at $p$ in vacuum, $\delta A = -\int R_{\mu\nu}\, k^\mu k^\nu\, \lambda\, d\lambda\, dA$.
  3. The local Unruh temperature is $T = \hbar\kappa / (2\pi k_B)$, where $\kappa$ is the surface gravity of the local Rindler horizon.
  4. Setting $\delta Q = T\, \delta S = T\, \eta\, \delta A$ and demanding it hold for all choices of $k^\mu$ at all points, you get a pointwise tensor equation: $T_{\mu\nu}\, k^\mu k^\nu \;\propto\; R_{\mu\nu}\, k^\mu k^\nu$ for all null $k^\mu$.
  5. The only tensor identity compatible with this for all null $k^\mu$ is the Einstein equation (modulo a piece proportional to $g_{\mu\nu}$, which becomes the cosmological constant).
Reading the actual 4-page PRL once you have the energy is worth it. It's startlingly short for a result this consequential.
Why this is hard Read the sentence again. Jacobson is saying: the field equations of gravity follow from thermodynamics applied to local horizons. To grasp why this should disturb you, an analogy.

The ideal gas law $PV = NkT$ is not a fundamental microscopic law. It is a thermodynamic consequence of the statistical mechanics of many molecules. Pressure and temperature are coarse-grained variables — they only make sense in the thermodynamic limit of many particles. Trying to "quantize pressure" (write $\hat P$ as an operator on a Hilbert space) would be misguided; pressure is not a fundamental quantum variable, it's an emergent statistical one.

Jacobson is suggesting that the spacetime metric $g_{\mu\nu}$ may be exactly the same kind of variable — a coarse-grained, hydrodynamic description of some underlying microstates we don't yet know. If that is right, then the entire enterprise of "quantizing GR" (canonical quantization, path-integral quantization, loop quantum gravity, even perturbative string theory) is in some sense quantizing the wrong thing — like quantizing pressure. That is what makes the sentence important.
Modern view The "metric is thermodynamic" perspective has driven a large fraction of theoretical-physics research since ~2000:
  • The holographic principle ('t Hooft 1993, Susskind 1995): the underlying microstates of a region of spacetime live on its boundary, with one bit per Planck area.
  • AdS/CFT (Maldacena 1997): a concrete realization — gravity in $(d+1)$-dim AdS = a conformal field theory on its $d$-dim boundary. The CFT is the "underlying microstates"; gravity is the thermodynamic / large-N limit.
  • Van Raamsdonk 2010: "building up spacetime from entanglement." In holographic settings, the geometry of the bulk emerges from entanglement structure in the boundary CFT. Disentangle the CFT, the bulk falls apart.
  • ER = EPR (Maldacena–Susskind 2013): entangled qubits glue spacetime together. Two black holes in an entangled state are connected by a wormhole (the Einstein–Rosen bridge). The conjecture: all entanglement creates wormhole-like geometric connectivity.
  • HaPPY codes (Pastawski–Yoshida–Harlow–Preskill 2015): a precise quantum error correction realization of holographic geometry. This is genuinely CS-shaped physics. See memory: project-ai-angle.
  • The islands formula and replica wormholes (Penington 2019; Almheiri–Engelhardt–Marolf–Maxfield 2019; Penington–Shenker–Stanford–Yang 2019): the modern resolution of the Hawking information paradox. The entire machinery presupposes that spacetime is emergent from entanglement, and computes the Page curve in that framework.
None of this is settled — "emergent gravity" is a research program, not an established result. But the sentence Jacobson tosses off in 2003 is the conceptual seed of an enormous body of contemporary work. When you eventually get to the path-3 / pure-theory destination (see memory: feedback-paper-strategy), this lineage is where the action is.
Context — for your trajectory This is also the kind of result that your CS/AI background reads more naturally than most physicists' GR background does. Coarse-graining, equations of state, the relationship between micro and macro descriptions, the structure of error-correcting codes — these are all native CS-shaped intuitions. The "emergent gravity from thermodynamics" research program is one of the genuine 2↔3 bridges mentioned in your project memory. Flag this passage. We will come back to it many times.

§J1.2 — What is a "horizon" here? Three kinds, with figures.

The word "horizon" gets used for several different objects in physics that look similar but live in different settings. Jacobson's argument uses a specific kind — the local Rindler horizon — and it helps to see it next to the more familiar kinds.

Kind 1 — Rindler horizon (flat spacetime)

The cleanest example: a uniformly accelerating observer in flat spacetime has a horizon. No black hole, no curvature, no GR. The observer's worldline is a hyperbola; the asymptote of that hyperbola is a 45° null line; events on the far side of that null line can never signal the observer.

Context — why this matters Even in flat spacetime, an accelerating observer has a horizon. The Unruh effect says they see the vacuum as thermal at $T = \hbar a/(2\pi k_B c)$. This is the conceptual seed of the whole local-Rindler-horizon idea: a horizon doesn't require a black hole, it just requires acceleration. And acceleration is something you can always invoke at any point of any spacetime.

Kind 2 — Local Rindler horizon (Jacobson's construction)

Now generalize. At any point $p$ in any spacetime (Schwarzschild, FLRW, your own messy numerical metric — anything), the equivalence principle tells us that the spacetime looks flat in a small neighborhood of $p$. So we can locally repeat the Rindler construction: imagine an observer accelerating through $p$, in some chosen direction. Their horizon — a small patch of null surface through $p$ — is the local Rindler horizon at $p$.

Translation In the math of Jacobson's argument, the local Rindler horizon is described by:
  • $p$ — the chosen point
  • $k^\mu$ — null vector at $p$, tangent to the chosen horizon patch (it points along the patch's null direction)
  • $\lambda$ — affine parameter measuring position along the horizon patch
  • $\kappa$ — surface gravity (how hard a hovering observer must accelerate to stay just outside the patch)
The thick orange segment in Figure J1.2-B is the part you compute $\delta Q$ and $\delta A$ over. The thermodynamic relation $\delta Q = T \, \delta S$ is enforced on this patch — and then on every other choice of $p$ and $k^\mu$. That universality is what forces the Einstein equation everywhere.

Kind 3 — Black hole event horizon (for comparison)

For completeness, the most famous horizon: the event horizon of a black hole. This is the kind Hawking 1975 computes radiation from. Note: it is global (defined by the entire future of spacetime), not local. Jacobson's argument does not use this kind — though it explains why this kind has a temperature.

Why Jacobson uses Kind 2, not Kind 3 The black hole event horizon (Kind 3) is global — to know if you're inside it, you'd need to know the entire future of the spacetime. That makes it useless for deriving a local field equation like Einstein's. The local Rindler horizon (Kind 2) is constructible pointwise — at every point of every spacetime, with no global information. That's what lets Jacobson's argument produce a tensor identity that holds pointwise, which is exactly the form Einstein's equation needs. The choice of Kind 2 over Kind 3 is the technical hinge of the entire derivation.

§A0 — The abstract

Three sentences. Each carries a lot of weight. We read them one at a time.

"In the classical theory black holes can only absorb and not emit particles. However it is shown that quantum mechanical effects cause black holes to create and emit particles as if they were hot bodies with temperature $\hbar\kappa/2\pi k$, where $\kappa$ is the surface gravity of the black hole."

Hawking 1975, abstract, p. 199
Translation Hawking's $\hbar\kappa/2\pi k$ is, in modern notation, the Hawking temperature $T_H = \hbar\kappa / (2\pi k_B c)$. Hawking sets $c = 1$ throughout the paper, and his $k$ is what we now write $k_B$ (Boltzmann's constant). For a Schwarzschild black hole of mass $M$, the surface gravity is $\kappa = c^4/(4 G M)$, so $T_H = \hbar c^3 / (8\pi G M k_B)$ — the famous form you see quoted in textbooks.
Context "Classically" here means: in classical general relativity. In classical GR, the event horizon is a one-way membrane — anything that tried to cross it outbound would have to exceed the speed of light. So classical black holes are perfect absorbers; their emission rate is identically zero. Hawking is announcing that this changes when you turn on quantum mechanics. Specifically: quantum-mechanical fields propagating on a classical curved background. The full quantization of gravity itself is something Hawking is deliberately not doing. This is "semi-classical gravity" — and 50 years later, this is still the only regime in which we can compute Hawking radiation.
Why this is hard The conceptual leap this sentence asks you to take is large. "But black holes are defined to be one-way membranes — how can anything come out?" is a reasonable question that you may need to sit with for hours. The answer, which the paper spends 20 pages delivering, is roughly: "vacuum" in a curved spacetime is not the same thing as "vacuum" in flat space. Observers in different regions of the spacetime disagree about which state is the vacuum, and that disagreement, made quantitative, is the particle creation. You do not have to understand the answer from the abstract. You just have to register that there is a real question being asked.

"This thermal emission leads to a slow decrease in the mass of the black hole and to its eventual disappearance: any primordial black hole of mass less than about $10^{15}$ g would have evaporated by now."

Hawking 1975, abstract, p. 199
Modern view This sentence quietly assumes the back-reaction is well-behaved: that the emitted radiation carries away energy, and therefore the black hole shrinks. Hawking is calculating on a fixed background and then arguing semi-heuristically from energy conservation that the mass must decrease. The full back-reaction problem — how the metric responds to the outgoing flux of (effectively negative) energy at the horizon — is much harder, and was not solved in this paper. Modern work on the information paradox (Page 1993, the islands formula of 2019+, replica wormholes) directly engages with this evolution. We don't need any of that yet. Just register: this sentence has hidden machinery.
Skipped step The "$10^{15}$ g" threshold comes from setting the evaporation time $\tau \sim G^2 M^3 / (\hbar c^4)$ equal to the age of the universe ($\sim 4 \times 10^{17}$ s). The cubic mass dependence comes from the Stefan–Boltzmann scaling $dM/dt \propto -T_H^4 \cdot A_{\rm BH} \propto -1/M^2$, which integrates to $\tau \propto M^3$. Hawking neither derives nor explains this number in the abstract — it lands as fact. Work it out on paper sometime; it is a clean dimensional-analysis exercise.

"Although these quantum effects violate the classical law that the area of the event horizon of a black hole cannot decrease, there remains a Generalized Second Law: $S + \tfrac{1}{4}A$ never decreases where $S$ is the entropy of matter outside black holes and $A$ is the sum of the surface areas of the event horizons."

Hawking 1975, abstract, p. 199
Context The "classical law that the area of the event horizon cannot decrease" is Hawking's own area theorem (1971). He is announcing in this very paper that his earlier theorem is violated by his new result — in the regime where quantum mechanics matters. The Generalized Second Law (GSL) is the conjectured replacement: ordinary thermodynamic entropy plus a quarter of the horizon area, in natural units, is non-decreasing.
Outdated Hawking writes "$\tfrac{1}{4}A$" without specifying units. In modern notation, "$\tfrac{1}{4}A$" means $A / (4 \ell_P^2)$ where $\ell_P = \sqrt{G\hbar / c^3}$ is the Planck length. The full dimensionful formula is the Bekenstein–Hawking entropy $$S_{\rm BH} = \frac{k_B c^3 A}{4 G \hbar}\,.$$ Hawking is using $G = c = \hbar = k_B = 1$ implicitly throughout most of the paper, but only states it inconsistently. Whenever a formula looks dimensionally suspect, the resolution is "natural units."
Modern view The status of the GSL is much more nuanced than Hawking made it look in 1975. There are proofs in restricted settings (Wall 2010, Bousso–Engelhardt 2015), and the islands/replica-wormhole work of 2019+ provides a holographic derivation in AdS-like settings that is the closest thing we have to a definitive resolution. None of this changes the formula Hawking writes; it just settles when and why it holds.

§A1.1 — The opening framing: what is "semi-classical gravity"?

The body of §1 begins (p. 199, after the abstract) by setting up the regime of validity for the calculation. Three paragraphs of Hawking framing what he is and is not doing.

"Although there has been a lot of work in the last fifteen years [...], I think it would be fair to say that we do not yet have a fully satisfactory and consistent quantum theory of gravity. At the moment classical General Relativity still provides the most successful description of gravity."

Hawking 1975, §1, p. 199
Context "The last fifteen years" puts the start of serious quantum-gravity work at around 1960 — DeWitt's papers on canonical quantization, the ADM formulation, etc. Fifty years after Hawking wrote this, the situation is essentially unchanged: we still do not have a fully satisfactory quantum theory of gravity. String theory and loop quantum gravity are the two longest-running programs; neither has delivered a complete dynamical theory in 4 dimensions that everyone agrees on. So Hawking's opening caveat is still binding. The entire Hawking-radiation calculation lives in the semi-classical regime by necessity, not by choice.

"The approximation I shall use in this paper is that the matter fields, such as scalar, electro-magnetic, or neutrino fields, obey the usual wave equations with the Minkowski metric replaced by a classical space-time metric $g_{ab}$. This metric satisfies the Einstein equations where the source on the right hand side is taken to be the expectation value of some suitably defined energy momentum operator for the matter fields."

Hawking 1975, §1, p. 199
Translation In modern language, Hawking is announcing he will work in quantum field theory in curved spacetime (QFTCS), coupled to the semi-classical Einstein equation $$G_{\mu\nu} = 8\pi G\,\langle \hat T_{\mu\nu}\rangle\,.$$ Matter is quantum (operator-valued fields $\hat\phi$, $\hat A_\mu$, etc.), spacetime is classical (a c-number metric $g_{\mu\nu}$), and they are coupled only through the expectation value of the stress tensor. Birrell & Davies (1982), still the canonical textbook on this, was written precisely to make this framework rigorous.
Outdated Hawking uses the abstract-index notation $g_{ab}$ (Latin indices) throughout the paper, following Penrose. Modern conventions usually use Greek indices $g_{\mu\nu}$ for spacetime indices. When you see $g_{ab}$, $K^a$, $T_{ab}$, etc. in Hawking, mentally translate to $g_{\mu\nu}$, $K^\mu$, $T_{\mu\nu}$. Nothing else changes; this is pure cosmetic.
Why this is hard The "approximation" Hawking describes here sounds innocent. It is not. The statement "spacetime is classical, matter is quantum, they are coupled through $\langle \hat T_{\mu\nu}\rangle$" hides three separate hard problems: (i) Which state are you taking the expectation value in? (Different vacua give different $\langle \hat T_{\mu\nu}\rangle$.) (ii) How do you regularize $\langle \hat T_{\mu\nu}\rangle$, which is formally divergent? (iii) When is this semi-classical coupling a good approximation to whatever the full quantum theory of gravity would say? Hawking is not going to answer any of these. He is going to assume the approximation is OK in his regime and compute.

"However one would hope that it would be a very good approximation for most purposes except near space-time singularities."

Hawking 1975, §1, p. 199
Modern view This "hope" was vindicated for most of the calculation but not at the very last stage: when a black hole evaporates to near-Planckian mass, the semiclassical approximation must break down. Modern work (page-curve, islands, etc.) finds that semiclassical gravity is actually more reliable than Hawking estimated for the early part of evaporation, but less reliable for understanding the final state of the radiation (the information paradox). Hawking's optimism here is in fact wrong in detail, but in a much more interesting way than he anticipated.

§A1.2 — The mode expansion (Hawking's Eq. 1.1)

Hawking now sets up the standard mode-decomposition of a quantum field in flat space, before extending it to curved spacetime. This is machinery you must own cold — every line of §2 of the paper assumes you can read this fluently.

"For example, if $\phi$ is a massless Hermitian scalar field obeying the equation $\phi_{;ab}\, g^{ab} = 0$ one expresses $\phi$ as" $$\phi = \sum_i \{f_i\, a_i + \bar f_i\, a_i^\dagger\} \quad\text{(1.1)}$$ "where the $\{f_i\}$ are a complete orthonormal family of complex valued solutions of the wave equation $f_{i;ab}\, g^{ab} = 0$ which contain only positive frequencies with respect to the usual Minkowski time coordinate."

Hawking 1975, §1, Eq. (1.1), p. 199–200
Translation In modern notation, the field operator is $\hat\phi = \sum_i (f_i \hat a_i + f_i^*\, \hat a_i^\dagger)$. The "$\phi_{;ab}\, g^{ab} = 0$" is just Hawking's covariant way of writing $\Box \phi = 0$ — the Klein–Gordon equation. His "$f_{;ab}\, g^{ab}$" is $\Box f$. (The semicolon is the covariant derivative; double semicolon-index $;ab$ is $\nabla_a \nabla_b$; contracted with $g^{ab}$ gives the d'Alembertian.)
Outdated Hawking writes $\bar f_i$ for the complex conjugate (the Penrose-style "bar"); modern texts more often write $f_i^*$. Hawking also doesn't put hats on his operators: his "$\phi$, $a_i$, $a_i^\dagger$" are all operators, but he uses the same letters as for c-number functions. When in doubt: if it appears in a sum with creation/annihilation operators, it's a function; if it has a dagger, it's an operator; if it has neither and is to the left of a $|0\rangle$, you have to read from context. Modern conventions consistently hat operators ($\hat\phi$, $\hat a$, $\hat a^\dagger$), which removes the ambiguity.
Context "Positive frequency with respect to the Minkowski time coordinate" means: $f_i(t, \vec x) \propto e^{-i\omega t}$ with $\omega > 0$. The minus sign in $e^{-i\omega t}$ is the physics convention — positive-frequency modes go as $e^{-i\omega t}$, not $e^{+i\omega t}$ — and it is non-negotiable. (This sign convention is one of the most frequent sources of confusion when reading old papers.) "Why positive frequency?" Because the Hamiltonian $\hat H = \omega \hat a^\dagger \hat a$ has positive eigenvalues: the $\hat a$'s lower the energy, the $\hat a^\dagger$'s raise it. If you mode-expanded using positive-and-negative-frequency modes mixed together, the $\hat a$ operators would no longer cleanly destroy quanta.
Skipped step Hawking does not explain what "complete orthonormal family" means here. He defines orthonormality in his Eq. (1.2) (next section, §A1.3). Completeness means the $\{f_i\}$ plus their conjugates $\{\bar f_i\}$ together span the full space of complex solutions to $\Box f = 0$. In flat space, the natural choice is plane waves $f_{\vec k}(t, \vec x) = (2\pi)^{-3/2} (2\omega_k)^{-1/2}\, e^{-i\omega_k t + i\vec k \cdot \vec x}$ with $\omega_k = |\vec k|$, so the discrete sum $\sum_i$ in Hawking's Eq. (1.1) is a stand-in for $\int d^3 k$. He keeps a discrete sum because, in the gravitational-collapse problem he is heading toward, the natural modes are not plane waves and the sum is over a curved-space mode index.

"The operators $a_i$ and $a_i^\dagger$ are interpreted as the annihilation and creation operators respectively for particles in the $i$th state. The vacuum state $|0\rangle$ is defined to be the state from which one cannot annihilate any particles, i.e. $a_i |0\rangle = 0$ for all $i$."

Hawking 1975, §1, p. 200
Why this is hard Watch this definition carefully — it is the entire pivot of the paper. The vacuum is defined relative to a choice of mode basis $\{f_i\}$. In flat space, that choice is essentially unique (Minkowski plane waves are the natural one). In curved spacetime, it is not unique — and the whole Hawking effect lives in that non-uniqueness. When Hawking later writes "$|0_-\rangle$ is the initial vacuum" (defined by the past-null-infinity modes $\{f_i\}$) and "$|0_+\rangle$ is the final vacuum" (defined by the future-null-infinity modes $\{p_i\}$), the fact that these are different states is the entire content of "particle creation." Anchor on this and the rest of §1 will click.

§A1.3 — The Klein–Gordon inner product (Hawking's Eq. 1.2)

"One could still require that the $\{f_i\}$ and the $\{\bar f_i\}$ together formed a complete basis for solutions of the wave equations with" $$-\frac{i}{2}\int_S\!\big(f_i\, \bar f_{j;a} - \bar f_{j}\, f_{i;a}\big)\, d\Sigma^a \;=\; \delta_{ij} \quad\text{(1.2)}$$ "where $S$ is a suitable surface."

Hawking 1975, §1, Eq. (1.2), p. 200
Translation This is the Klein–Gordon inner product. In modern notation, $$\langle f, g\rangle_{\rm KG} \;\equiv\; -i \int_\Sigma\, \left( f\, \partial_\mu g^* - g^*\, \partial_\mu f \right)\, n^\mu\, d\Sigma\,,$$ where $\Sigma$ is a spacelike Cauchy surface and $n^\mu$ is its future-directed unit normal. Hawking's compact form "$\int_S (f_i\, \bar f_{j;a} - \bar f_j\, f_{i;a}) d\Sigma^a$" packages the volume element $d\Sigma$ and the normal $n^\mu$ into a single oriented surface element $d\Sigma^a = n^a d\Sigma$, and uses the covariant derivative semicolon notation in place of $\partial_\mu$ (they agree on a scalar field).
Context Why this particular formula? Because $\langle f, g\rangle_{\rm KG}$ is conserved: if $f$ and $g$ both solve the Klein–Gordon equation, the integral is independent of which spacelike surface $\Sigma$ you evaluate it on. (You can prove this from $\Box f = 0$ and integration by parts; it is one of those exercises everyone eventually does and that physicists almost never write down.) That conservation is what makes "positive-frequency" a well-defined labeling: a mode that's positive-frequency on one slice stays positive-frequency on every Cauchy slice in flat space.
Skipped step Hawking does not flag a subtlety: positive-frequency modes have $\langle f_i, f_j\rangle_{\rm KG} = +\delta_{ij}$, but negative-frequency modes have $\langle \bar f_i, \bar f_j\rangle_{\rm KG} = -\delta_{ij}$. The Klein–Gordon "inner product" is not positive-definite; it is indefinite. This is exactly what allows positive and negative frequencies to mix under a Bogoliubov transformation (next section). If you have studied special relativity, this should remind you of the indefiniteness of the Minkowski metric — and it is essentially the same structure.
Why this is hard The "KG inner product" is treated as obvious in Hawking's paper and in most graduate-level QFT-in-curved-spacetime presentations. It is not obvious. If you have only seen quantization in non-relativistic quantum mechanics or in canonical (Hamiltonian) flat-space QFT, you have probably never written this object down explicitly. Spend an hour with Birrell & Davies §3.2 if you can; they walk through the derivation slowly. Or with Jacobson §4.2 of his notes (Jacobson 2003, p. 13–14). It is worth the hour. Every later equation in Hawking 1975 uses this inner product implicitly.

Lecture 1 From Hawking 1975 to one greybody factor drafted

Prerequisite: Hawking 1975 (skimmed at minimum) Outcome: you know what equation to integrate, with what boundary conditions, to extract one number

1.1 — Where we are, and what this lecture is for

You've just spent months with Hawking 1975. In his own opening words:

"In the classical theory black holes can only absorb and not emit particles. However it is shown that quantum mechanical effects cause black holes to create and emit particles as if they were hot bodies with temperature $\hbar\kappa / 2\pi k$, where $\kappa$ is the surface gravity of the black hole."

— Hawking 1975, abstract, p. 199

For a Schwarzschild black hole the surface gravity is $\kappa = 1/(4M)$ (units $G = c = 1$), so $$T_H \;=\; \frac{\hbar \kappa}{2\pi k_B} \;=\; \frac{\hbar c^3}{8\pi G M k_B}\,.$$ But — and this is the part Hawking spells out only at the end of §2 of the paper — that flux is not exactly Planckian. Each mode is modulated by a frequency-dependent factor that encodes how much of an outgoing wave actually escapes the curvature potential outside the horizon. Hawking's final formula for the number of particles created in a given outgoing wave-packet mode is $$\boxed{\;\; F_{j n}\,\bigl(\exp(2\pi\omega/\kappa) - 1\bigr)^{-1}\;\;}$$ — Hawking 1975, Eq. (2.29), p. 211

where Hawking's $F_{jn}$ is, in his own words, "the fraction of the wave-packet that would have crossed the past event horizon". That fraction is the greybody factor. Modern notation calls it $\Gamma_{\omega\ell}$; Jacobson writes it as the norm $\langle T, T \rangle$ of the transmitted wavepacket (Jacobson 2003, Eq. (6.26), p. 38). Different names, same number: the fraction of an outgoing mode of frequency $\omega$ and angular momentum $\ell$ that survives the curvature barrier.

The problem: you've read the paper, but you don't know what to compute. Hawking's mode-decomposition language doesn't tell you which Python program to write tomorrow. This lecture's only job is to bridge that gap. By the end you'll know exactly which equation to integrate, with which boundary conditions, to extract a single number $\Gamma_{\omega\ell}$ you can match against Page 1976.

1.2 — Hawking's own argument, in his own equations

Hawking expands the scalar field operator $\hat\phi$ in two equivalent ways. First, in terms of modes $\{f_i\}$ that are positive-frequency on past null infinity $\mathcal{I}^-$: $$\hat\phi \;=\; \sum_i \bigl(f_i\, \hat a_i + \bar f_i\, \hat a_i^\dagger\bigr)\,,$$ — Hawking 1975, Eq. (2.3) and second, in terms of modes $\{p_i\}$ that are purely outgoing (positive-frequency on future null infinity $\mathcal{I}^+$) plus modes $\{q_i\}$ that fall into the horizon: $$\hat\phi \;=\; \sum_i \bigl(p_i\, \hat b_i + \bar p_i\, \hat b_i^\dagger + q_i\, \hat c_i + \bar q_i\, \hat c_i^\dagger\bigr)\,.$$ — Hawking 1975, Eq. (2.4)

The two bases are related by linear combinations, $p_i = \sum_j (\alpha_{ij} f_j + \beta_{ij} \bar f_j)$ (Hawking Eq. (2.5)), which gives the corresponding Bogoliubov relation between operators, $\hat b_i = \sum_j (\bar\alpha_{ij} \hat a_j - \bar\beta_{ij} \hat a_j^\dagger)$ (Hawking Eq. (2.7)). Now the initial vacuum $|0_-\rangle$ — the state with no incoming particles on $\mathcal{I}^-$ — is defined by $\hat a_i |0_-\rangle = 0$. But the expectation value of the number of outgoing particles in mode $i$, as measured at $\mathcal{I}^+$, is $$\langle 0_- | \hat b_i^\dagger \hat b_i | 0_- \rangle \;=\; \sum_j |\beta_{ij}|^2\,.$$ — Hawking 1975, Eq. (2.10), p. 206

This is the entire problem in one line: nonzero $\beta_{ij}$ means particle creation. Whatever later formal machinery Hawking deploys — and the rest of §2 of the paper is a tour of it — the question being answered is "what is $|\beta_{\omega\omega'}|^2$?"

The unmotivated cleverness of the paper, which Hawking states but doesn't dwell on, is this: once the collapse has settled, the geometry outside the event horizon is stationary. The wave equation for late-time outgoing modes lives entirely on this stationary background. The Bogoliubov coefficients $\beta_{\omega\omega'}$ become, in the late-time regime, the scattering amplitudes of a 1D wave equation on a fixed effective potential. So the "Hawking calculation," for the part you actually compute on a laptop, collapses to:

Solve a 1D Schrödinger-like equation on Schwarzschild. Extract its transmission coefficient.

That is the entire program of §1.3–§1.6 below. Jacobson 2003 gives a clean modern derivation in §6.2 of his lecture notes — we'll follow his version because his notation is closer to what you'll write in code.

1.3 — The radial wave equation, following Jacobson

Write the Schwarzschild line element in the form $$ds^2 \;=\; \Bigl(1 - \frac{r_s}{r}\Bigr) dt^2 - \Bigl(1 - \frac{r_s}{r}\Bigr)^{-1} dr^2 - r^2 (d\theta^2 + \sin^2\theta\, d\varphi^2)\,,$$ — Jacobson 2003, Eq. (6.15), p. 35 where $r_s = 2GM$ is the Schwarzschild radius. Define the tortoise coordinate by $$dr_* \;=\; \frac{dr}{1 - r_s/r}\,,\qquad r_* \;=\; r + r_s \ln\!\left(\frac{r}{r_s} - 1\right),$$ which goes to $-\infty$ at the horizon ($r \to r_s$) and to $+\infty$ at spatial infinity. Decompose the field into spherical harmonics, $$\phi(t,r,\theta,\varphi) \;=\; \sum_{\ell m} \frac{\varphi_{\ell m}(t,r)}{r}\, Y_{\ell m}(\theta,\varphi)\,,$$ — Jacobson 2003, Eq. (6.18) and the Klein–Gordon equation $(\Box + m^2)\phi = 0$ reduces to the 1+1-dimensional equation

$$\boxed{\;\; \bigl(\partial_t^2 - \partial_{r_*}^2 + V_{\ell m}\bigr)\,\varphi_{\ell m} \;=\; 0 \;\;}$$ — Jacobson 2003, Eq. (6.19), p. 36 with the effective potential $$\boxed{\;\; V_{\ell m}(r) \;=\; \Bigl(1 - \frac{r_s}{r}\Bigr)\!\left[\,\frac{r_s}{r^3} + \frac{\ell(\ell+1)}{r^2} + m^2\,\right] \;\;}$$ — Jacobson 2003, Eq. (6.20), p. 36

For the massless scalar ($m = 0$) and using $r_s = 2M$: $$V_\ell(r) \;=\; \left(1 - \frac{2M}{r}\right) \! \left[ \frac{\ell(\ell+1)}{r^2} + \frac{2M}{r^3} \right]\,.$$ That second term — $2M/r^3$ — is sometimes startling. It comes from the fact that we factored out $1/r$ when defining $\varphi_{\ell m} = r \, \phi_{\ell m}$, and that the d'Alembertian on Schwarzschild generates a residual curvature contribution. Plotting $V_\ell$ for $\ell = 0, 1, 2$ on Schwarzschild is your first piece of code — see §1.4.

For positive Killing frequency $\omega$, factor $\varphi_{\ell m}(t, r) = e^{-i\omega t}\, \psi_{\omega\ell}(r_*)$. The time-independent radial equation is then $$\frac{d^2 \psi_{\omega\ell}}{dr_*^2} + \bigl[\,\omega^2 - V_\ell(r)\,\bigr]\, \psi_{\omega\ell} \;=\; 0\,.$$ This is a 1D Schrödinger equation with energy $\omega^2$ scattering off the curvature potential $V_\ell$. The greybody factor is its transmission coefficient.

Exercise 1.1. Derive Jacobson's Eq. (6.20) from the Klein–Gordon equation on Schwarzschild. The algebra is about half a page. The key step is that factoring out $1/r$ in $\phi = \varphi/r \cdot Y_{\ell m}$ generates the extra $r_s/r^3$ term from the radial derivatives — work through $\partial_r(r \cdot \ldots) = r \partial_r + 1$ pieces carefully. Do this on paper before reading on. If you can do this cleanly, you understand 90% of what Hawking compresses into the half-sentence "the operator $\hat\phi$ can be expressed as $\sum_i \{\ldots\}$ everywhere" after Eq. (2.4) of his paper.

1.4 — What does the potential look like?

$V_\ell(r)$ vanishes at the horizon $r = 2M$ (because $f(2M) = 0$) and at $r \to \infty$ (because everything inside the brackets falls off). In between it has a single maximum near $r \sim 3M$ — the photon sphere for $\ell \gg 1$ — whose height grows with $\ell$.

This barrier is what makes the spectrum greybody rather than perfectly Planckian. Hawking quanta are born "behind" the barrier, near the horizon, with some frequency $\omega$. To reach infinity they have to tunnel through (for $\omega^2 < V_{\rm max}$) or pass over (for $\omega^2 > V_{\rm max}$) this barrier. $\Gamma_{\omega\ell}$ is the fraction that gets through.

Your first piece of code will simply plot $V_\ell(r)$ vs. $r$ for $\ell = 0, 1, 2, 3$. Three lines of NumPy. Do it before the next section.

1.4.5 — Aside: Hawking's logarithmic phase pile-up

The reason it takes Hawking the bulk of §2 to get to Eq. (2.29) is that his argument doesn't use the scattering language we've just set up. He uses geometric optics: he traces an outgoing wavepacket at $\mathcal{I}^+$ backwards through the collapsing geometry to $\mathcal{I}^-$, and asks what frequency it had there.

The central step (Hawking p. 208–209, his Eqs. (2.16)–(2.18)) is to show that on $\mathcal{I}^-$, the back-propagated wave develops an infinite number of cycles piling up at a critical advanced time $v_0$ — the latest time a null geodesic could escape to $\mathcal{I}^+$ before being trapped by the forming horizon. Specifically, the phase of his solution $p_\omega^{(2)}$ on $\mathcal{I}^-$ for $v_0 - v$ small and positive is $$\frac{\omega}{\kappa}\,\bigl(\log(v_0 - v) - \log D - \log C\bigr)\,,$$ — Hawking 1975, Eq. (2.17), p. 209 so the wavepacket on $\mathcal{I}^-$ looks like $$p_\omega^{(2)} \;\simeq\; \frac{P_\omega^0}{(2\pi)^{1/2}}\, \omega^{-1/2}\, r^{-1}\, \exp\!\left[ -\frac{i\omega}{\kappa} \log\!\left( \frac{v_0 - v}{CD} \right) \right]\,.$$ — Hawking 1975, Eq. (2.18), p. 209

That logarithmic phase is the entire game. Fourier-transforming it gives $\alpha_{\omega\omega'}$ and $\beta_{\omega\omega'}$ as gamma functions of $\omega/\kappa$, and the ratio that controls particle creation is $$|\alpha_{\omega\omega'}^{(2)}| \;=\; \exp(\pi\omega/\kappa)\,|\beta_{\omega\omega'}^{(2)}|\,,$$ — Hawking 1975, Eq. (2.21), p. 209 which squares to the Planck factor $|\beta|^2 / |\alpha|^2 = e^{-2\pi\omega/\kappa}$. Modulating by the absorption probability of an analogous incoming wave-packet gives Hawking's Eq. (2.29) — the formula we already met in §1.1.

Why we are not computing this way. Hawking's geometric-optics derivation is conceptually beautiful but numerically awful: it requires tracing wave-packets through trans-Planckian frequencies near the horizon, which is exactly the "trans-Planckian problem" Jacobson discusses in §7 of his notes. The scattering formulation in §1.5–§1.6 below sidesteps this entirely — you compute the transmission coefficient $|T_{\omega\ell}|^2$ of a 1D wave equation on a fixed potential, which is the same quantity Hawking's $F_{jn}$ measures but reached without ever invoking high-frequency modes near the horizon. The two derivations agree on $\Gamma_{\omega\ell}$; only the second is something you can put in scipy.integrate.solve_ivp.

1.5 — The scattering boundary conditions

We want the solution that describes Hawking radiation: something born at the horizon, propagating outward, scattering off $V_\ell$, with a transmitted piece reaching infinity. At the same time, by reversibility, we equivalently study the time-reversed problem: a unit-amplitude wave coming in from infinity, partially reflected, partially absorbed by the horizon. We use the time-reversed form because it is computationally cleaner.

The scattering boundary conditions are:

$$\psi_{\omega\ell}(r_*) \xrightarrow{r_* \to -\infty}\; T_{\omega\ell}\, e^{-i \omega r_*}\,,$$ $$\psi_{\omega\ell}(r_*) \xrightarrow{r_* \to +\infty}\; e^{-i \omega r_*} + R_{\omega\ell}\, e^{+i \omega r_*}\,.$$

In words: at the horizon, only ingoing waves (nothing comes out of the horizon classically); at infinity, an incident unit-amplitude wave plus a reflected wave. The complex numbers $T_{\omega\ell}$ and $R_{\omega\ell}$ are the transmission and reflection amplitudes.

Probability conservation (the Wronskian of the radial equation is constant) forces $|T|^2 + |R|^2 = 1$. The greybody factor is

$$\boxed{\quad \Gamma_{\omega\ell} \;=\; |T_{\omega\ell}|^2 \;=\; 1 - |R_{\omega\ell}|^2 \quad}$$ — compare Jacobson 2003, Eq. (6.26), p. 38: $\Gamma = \langle T, T \rangle$

Jacobson's $\langle T, T \rangle$ in Eq. (6.26) is the Klein–Gordon norm of the part of an outgoing wavepacket at infinity that, when propagated backwards in time, ends up pressed against the horizon (rather than scattering back out to infinity). That is exactly the same number — by time-reversal — as $|T_{\omega\ell}|^2$ in our forward-in-time scattering setup. Pick whichever picture makes the geometry clear in your head. The number you compute is the same.

Exercise 1.2. Compute the Wronskian $W = \psi \, d\psi^* / dr_* - \psi^*\, d\psi / dr_*$ for two solutions of the radial equation, show it's conserved, and use it to derive $|T|^2 + |R|^2 = 1$. Half-page exercise. Do it on paper. The Wronskian relation is also what makes the numerical extraction of $T$ robust — see §1.6.

1.6 — How to extract one number

The numerical recipe — the thing you will implement tomorrow — is the "shoot from the horizon" method:

  1. Pick numerical values: $M = 1$ (units), $\ell = 0$, $\omega M = 0.25$ (a value tabulated by Page 1976 for direct comparison).
  2. Choose a starting radius just outside the horizon, say $r_{\rm start}/M = 2 + 10^{-3}$, and the corresponding $r_*^{\rm start}$. Impose the purely-ingoing boundary condition there, $\psi(r_*^{\rm start}) = e^{-i\omega r_*^{\rm start}}$ and $d\psi/dr_*(r_*^{\rm start}) = -i\omega\, e^{-i\omega r_*^{\rm start}}$.
  3. Integrate the radial equation outward in $r_*$ using scipy.integrate.solve_ivp with a tight tolerance (rtol=1e-10, atol=1e-12), with the equation cast as a 2D first-order system $(\psi, \psi') \mapsto (\psi', V\psi - \omega^2 \psi)$.
  4. Stop at a large radius, $r_{\rm end}/M \approx 200$. At that point $V_\ell(r) \ll \omega^2$ and the solution looks like $\psi \approx A_+\, e^{+i\omega r_*} + A_-\, e^{-i\omega r_*}$. Solve the 2×2 linear system for $(A_+, A_-)$ given $(\psi, \psi'/i\omega)$ at $r_{\rm end}$.
  5. Identify the transmission and reflection amplitudes by normalizing your boundary conditions: with the ingoing-at-horizon normalization above, $T_{\omega\ell} = 1 / A_-$ and $R_{\omega\ell} = A_+ / A_-$. Compute $\Gamma_{\omega\ell} = |T_{\omega\ell}|^2$.
  6. Compare to Page 1976, Table II, scalar entry at $\omega M = 0.25$, $\ell = 0$. Your number should agree to several digits.

That is the entire first deliverable. One number. Hours of work, not months. Once it matches Page, you have a numerical pipeline that — by changing the function $f(r)$ in the metric — will give you greybody factors for any spherically symmetric spacetime. The centerpiece paper is then a matter of choosing $f(r)$ wisely (see Lecture 4) and computing.

1.7 — Why this approach > reading more papers

An honest observation. The fastest way to make Hawking 1975 retroactively make sense is not to read it again. It's to do §1.6 above. After you've watched the radial equation evolve in your debugger, after you've seen the reflected-and-transmitted wave train at large $r$, the abstract Bogoliubov machinery in §3–4 of Hawking maps onto something physical you've now seen. The "logarithmic phase pile-up" that Hawking spends pages on is just what your wave looks like as you integrate close to the horizon.

Compute-then-understand is a perfectly respectable physicist's path — Fermi worked this way, Feynman worked this way, every numerical relativist alive today works this way. Read enough to get oriented, then compute, then re-read with the computation in your hands. The pure read-until-enlightened tradition is one path; the compute-then-understand tradition is another. Use whichever moves you forward.
