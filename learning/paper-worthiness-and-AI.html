Paper-Worthiness & AI in Research

When is a finding worth a paper? What papers should you read as models? And — honestly — where is paper-writing going given AI? Four parts.
13 modules · exemplar PDFs fetched to papers/exemplars/ · honest section on AI in Part III is not corporate

Module 1Five criteria, in order of importance

Almost all of what passes for "what makes a paper publishable" advice is vague. Here are the operational criteria. They are listed in order: a paper missing #1 cannot be saved by #2–#5; a paper missing #5 alone can sometimes still be published.

1. Novelty

The single mandatory criterion. A paper must contain a fact, a derivation, a method, a perspective, or a connection that did not exist in the literature before. "Novel" does not mean revolutionary — it means new. The bar is lower than people think, but it is sharp.

Types of novelty, in roughly decreasing power:

  1. A new theoretical framework that re-organizes a field (Maldacena 1997 — AdS/CFT).
  2. A new theorem or no-go result (Hawking's area theorem 1971).
  3. A new physical phenomenon predicted or discovered (Hawking radiation 1975).
  4. A new microscopic explanation of an established macroscopic result (Strominger–Vafa 1996 — counting BH microstates).
  5. A new calculation: solving a problem that had been open (any specific QNM or greybody calculation done numerically for the first time).
  6. A new method that improves an existing calculation (Leaver's continued-fraction method for QNMs, 1985).
  7. A new connection between two previously-disconnected results (the wide class of "X is dual to Y" papers).
  8. A reorganization or pedagogical reframing — only sometimes sufficient.

2. Soundness

The math must be right. The numerics must be reproducible. The argument must not contain logical gaps. Soundness is non-negotiable and is what referees check first. A paper with a beautiful idea but a wrong calculation is rejected.

3. Significance

The result must matter to other researchers. "Matter" is field-specific. In black hole physics: does it advance our understanding of horizons, of Hawking radiation, of the information paradox, of observational signatures, of theoretical structure? "Significance" is what determines journal tier (Nature → PRL → PRD → CQG → JHEP → specialized journals).

4. Reproducibility

Other people must be able to reproduce your work. For numerical papers this means: clear specification of method, accuracy targets, convergence tests, ideally a public code repository. For theoretical papers: all steps in the derivation, or a clear pointer to where they live.

5. Communicability

The paper must be readable. Mostly this means: clear writing, well-structured sections, equations that are explained, figures that stand alone. A paper that satisfies 1–4 but is unreadable will still be published (sometimes), but it will be cited less and your reputation will absorb the cost.

Module 2The minimum publishable unit

The "minimum publishable unit" (MPU) in physics is field-dependent but roughly:

FieldMPUTypical journal
Black hole physics (theory)One clean new calculation or frameworkPRD, CQG
String theory / formalOne non-trivial new derivation or dualityJHEP, PRD
Numerical relativityOne new numerical result or method improvementPRD, CQG
PhenomenologyOne specific prediction tied to observationPRD, JCAP
CosmologyOne new model or analysis of dataPRD, JCAP, ApJ
ExperimentOne measurement with stated uncertaintyPRD, PRL, Nature
ReviewSynthesis of a sub-field by invitationRev. Mod. Phys., Liv. Rev. Rel.

For your centerpiece paper (numerical greybody factors for a less-studied spacetime): the MPU is satisfied by one spacetime, one field species, $\Gamma_{\omega\ell}(\omega)$ tabulated for a useful range of parameters, and the integrated emission spectrum, with the result compared to Schwarzschild as a benchmark. That is enough for PRD.

Yes — paper
  • "We compute $\Gamma_{\omega\ell}(\omega)$ for the Hayward black hole, scalar field, $\ell=0..5$, and find suppression at low frequency consistent with the regular-core structure." Concrete, novel, reproducible.
  • "We propose a new derivation of $S = A/4$ using only entanglement of small causal diamonds." (Jacobson 2015.)
  • "We compute greybody factors for spacetime X using a PINN, demonstrating accuracy comparable to continued fractions at 10× the speed. Reproducible code released." Method paper.
No — not a paper
  • "We re-derive the Schwarzschild greybody factors using the standard method." No novelty.
  • "We propose that black holes are composed of stringy fuzz." No calculation, no soundness.
  • "We compute $\Gamma_{\omega\ell}$ for the Bardeen spacetime, but our results disagree with Rincón–Santos 2020 by orders of magnitude with no explanation." Likely unsound.
  • "We comment on a recent paper's typo." Unless the typo invalidates a major result.
Maybe — depends on framing
  • "We provide a pedagogical introduction to the Jacobson derivation accessible to early-career students." Possibly for AJP (American Journal of Physics).
  • "We solve a special case of a known problem cleanly." If the special case is interesting or the cleanness is itself the contribution.
  • "We extend a known result from $D=4$ to $D=5$." If the extension is non-trivial.

Module 3Six self-tests — apply before writing

  1. Can you write the abstract in 4 sentences right now? (Problem, method, result, why it matters.) If yes, you probably have a paper. If you struggle past the second sentence, you do not yet have a result; you have an aspiration.
  2. Can someone cite you for a specific reason? Imagine a future paper writing "$X$ shown that..." — what fills the blank? If you can't fill it cleanly, the contribution isn't sharp enough.
  3. What's the one number, one equation, or one plot that is the headline? Every paper has one. If you can't point to it, it doesn't exist yet.
  4. Can you defend the result at a seminar with five hostile questions? Imagine the questions. If they all have answers, you have a paper. If even one stumps you, fix it before writing.
  5. Has somebody done this already? Search arXiv with five different keyword combinations. If you find a paper that does what you're doing within $\epsilon$, you must either find your distinct angle or drop the project.
  6. Why would Hamming ask "why aren't you working on something more important?" Give the honest answer. If the answer is "because this is what I can finish in 6 months and it's a defensible stepping stone," that's fine. If the answer is "because I'm avoiding the harder thing I should be doing," reconsider.
Exercise

Right now, draft a 4-sentence abstract of your intended centerpiece paper. The four sentences:

  1. What is the question / open problem?
  2. What did you do?
  3. What did you find?
  4. Why does it matter?

If you cannot do this exercise, you don't have a paper yet. You have an intention. The first action item is to refine the question until you can.

Module 4Short classics — read for style

The best way to learn paper-writing is to study short, excellent papers as models. The papers below are all short (the longest is 22 pages). All have changed the field. Each one teaches a different lesson about craft.

Exemplar 4.1

Bekenstein, "Black holes and entropy," PRD 7, 2333 (1973). ~12 pages.

Why read it: the introduction of black hole entropy. A short, audacious paper that proposes one of the most important formulas in physics ($S = A/4$) on dimensional and information-theoretic grounds, before Hawking had any quantum derivation. The argument structure is principle-driven and clear.

What to copy: the way a result is motivated by a thought experiment (lowering a particle into a black hole) before any equations. Read for the argument's logical spine, not the math.

Access: PRD, paywalled. Library or university access. Not in your corpus.

Exemplar 4.2

Unruh, "Notes on black-hole evaporation," PRD 14, 870 (1976). ~22 pages.

Why read it: clarifies and sharpens Hawking 1975 with the Unruh effect. The paper is a model of how to extend a major result by isolating the simplest core argument.

What to copy: the way an existing famous result is re-derived from a cleaner starting point. This pattern — "here is a clearer way to see what was already known" — is a respectable paper genre in itself.

Access: PRD, paywalled.

Exemplar 4.3

Jacobson, "Thermodynamics of spacetime: The Einstein equation of state," PRL 75, 1260 (1995). 9 pages.

Why read it: the canonical short conceptual paper of the last 30 years. Four ingredients, one identity, the entire Einstein equation derived. PRL letter format forces extreme compression — and the paper benefits.

What to copy: the compression itself. Every paragraph carries weight. There is no padding. The conclusion arrives in 9 pages because every step is necessary.

Access: papers/exemplars/Jacobson1995_EinsteinEquationOfState.pdf (already on disk).

Module 5Numerical exemplars — your closest models

For a numerical first paper, the most directly relevant exemplars are pre-2000 PRD papers that established the templates of the genre.

Exemplar 5.1 — your direct template

Page, "Particle emission rates from a black hole: Massless particles from an uncharged, nonrotating hole," PRD 13, 198 (1976). ~9 pages.

Why read it: the canonical numerical-greybody paper. Computes $\Gamma_{\omega\ell}$ for Schwarzschild for scalar, photon, graviton — tabulated values that have been benchmarked against for fifty years. This is the paper your centerpiece paper is modeled on.

What to copy: (i) the structure — set up the problem, state the numerical method, present tables of results, end with the integrated emission spectrum. (ii) the discipline of providing tables that other people can benchmark against. (iii) the brevity — no padding.

Access: PRD, paywalled. Library access. The most important paper for you to read is the most awkward to obtain.

Exemplar 5.2 — method paper

Leaver, "An analytic representation for the quasinormal modes of Kerr black holes," Proc. Roy. Soc. A 402, 285 (1985). ~14 pages.

Why read it: the introduction of the continued-fraction method for QNMs. A method paper that has been the workhorse of numerical black hole physics for forty years.

What to copy: the architecture of a method paper. State the problem, derive the algorithm, demonstrate accuracy on known cases, apply to a new case. If your paper's contribution is partly methodological (e.g., a ML emulator alongside classical numerics), this is the structural template.

Access: Proc. Roy. Soc., paywalled.

Module 6Modern landmarks

Exemplar 6.1

Strominger & Vafa, "Microscopic origin of the Bekenstein–Hawking entropy," PLB 379, 99 (1996). 12 pages.

Why read it: a short paper that explicitly computes $S = A/4$ from D-brane microstate counting for a specific extremal black hole. The entire derivation in 12 pages. A masterclass in conveying a result with global implications via a single concrete calculation.

What to copy: the discipline of choosing the simplest non-trivial case that proves your point. The paper does not try to derive $S = A/4$ for all black holes; it derives it exactly for one. The general lesson follows from the example.

Access: papers/string_theory/StromingerVafa1996_BHEntropy.pdf.

Exemplar 6.2

Maldacena, "The large N limit of superconformal field theories and supergravity," Adv. Theor. Math. Phys. 2, 231 (1997). 22 pages.

Why read it: proposes AdS/CFT. The most-cited paper in theoretical physics since Einstein 1916. Worth studying as a model of how to propose a framework: the actual claim is hedged carefully, the evidence is laid out step by step, and the implications are sketched without being over-claimed.

What to copy: the way a sweeping claim is presented with carefully bounded evidence. The paper does not overclaim what it has proven.

Access: papers/string_theory/Maldacena1997_AdSCFT.pdf.

Exemplar 6.3 — modern format

Penington, "Entanglement wedge reconstruction and the information paradox," JHEP 09 (2020) 002, arXiv:1905.08255. ~75 pages.

Why read it: a modern long-form theoretical paper. One of two simultaneous papers that resurrected the information paradox after 40 years. Read for the structure of a long paper that nevertheless reads cleanly: a clear introduction that surveys the question, careful step-by-step setup of the calculation, an explicit "main result" boxed equation, extensive discussion of implications.

What to copy: how a long paper organizes its sections so a reader can navigate. The table of contents and roadmap paragraph in the introduction are tutorial-quality.

Access: papers/exemplars/Penington2019_EntanglementWedgeIslands.pdf.

Exemplar 6.4

Almheiri, Engelhardt, Marolf, Maxfield, "The entropy of bulk quantum fields and the entanglement wedge of an evaporating black hole," JHEP 12 (2019) 063, arXiv:1905.08762. ~55 pages.

Why read it: the parallel paper to Penington 2019, written by four authors. A good model of how multi-author papers carry distinct voices into a coherent presentation. Read for the introduction (model of a research-front review).

Access: papers/exemplars/AEMM2019_EntropyOfBulkQuantumFields.pdf.

Module 7How to study an exemplar — the three-pass method

Don't just read these papers. Study them as artifacts. Three-pass protocol:

Pass 1 — Architecture (20 min)

  • Read only: title, abstract, section headings, figures + captions, conclusion.
  • Question to answer: what is the overall claim? what is the headline result?
  • Write down in your own words, two sentences max.

Pass 2 — Argument spine (2 hours)

  • Read the introduction in full. Mark every paragraph with one of: motivation, prior work, contribution, roadmap.
  • Read the main calculation, skimming algebra. Identify the central inequality / equation / theorem.
  • Read the discussion carefully.
  • Question to answer: what is the structure of the argument? Could you sketch it in 5 bullets?

Pass 3 — Mechanics (1 day if you really want to learn)

  • Read every paragraph for craft, not for content. How is the equation set up before display? How is each figure introduced? How is prior work cited — by name and result, or by reference number only?
  • Note three things the paper does that you should adopt in your own writing.
  • Note one thing the paper does that you would do differently.

Do this for one of the short classics (Jacobson 1995 or Strominger–Vafa 1996) this week. The lessons compound across papers.

Module 8State of AI in research, mid-2026 — what's actually happening

You asked me to be honest. Here is the honest assessment, from the actor whose role you are asking about.

8.1 What physicists actually use AI for, today

  • Prose drafting and polishing. Nearly universal among non-native English speakers; majority of native speakers also. Drafting introductions, polishing phrasing, restructuring paragraphs. This is the dominant use.
  • Literature orientation. "Summarize the main results of arXiv:XXXX." "Find papers on Y by ABC." Useful but with frequent hallucinations on specifics.
  • Code generation. Drafting numerical-method scripts (shooting methods, FFTs, plot generation). Very effective for routine code.
  • Derivation drafting. Working through routine multi-line calculations with verification by the human. The human's role: check.
  • Concept explanation. "Explain Wilsonian renormalization in terms of this thing I already know." Effective tutoring.
  • Translation between formalisms. "Rewrite this in Penrose notation." "Convert this from $(-+++)$ to $(+---)$."

8.2 What journals require, as of 2026

  • Nature, Science: AI tools must be disclosed. AI cannot be listed as author. Authors are responsible for content.
  • arXiv: requires disclosure of "substantial use of generative AI tools." No specific list.
  • APS journals (PRL, PRD): disclosure required for substantive use; AI not an author.
  • The norm has settled: AI is a tool; you are accountable for what your paper says.

Module 9What AI is, and is not, good at — concretely

Genuinely good at
  • Cleaning up English prose, removing redundancy, tightening sentences.
  • Generating boilerplate (LaTeX preamble, BibTeX entries, common plot styles).
  • Drafting numerical code in standard libraries (SciPy, NumPy, matplotlib).
  • Identifying that a derivation is wrong because of a sign or factor of 2 (basic verification).
  • Summarizing a paper at the level of "what is it about, what did they do, what did they find" — when the paper is in its training data.
  • Helping a beginner navigate unfamiliar formalism by translating to language they know.
  • Recognizing common patterns: "this looks like a Bogoliubov transformation," "this resembles a CFT 4-point function."
Not good at — and confidently wrong
  • Knowing which problems are important. Physical taste is not in the training data; the field's evolving sense of what matters is.
  • Sustained multi-step novel reasoning. Will lose its thread in a calculation that requires holding many constraints simultaneously.
  • Recognizing when it has confabulated a citation, a formula, or a result. The output is fluent regardless of whether the content is real.
  • Distinguishing subtle valid arguments from subtle invalid ones — especially in areas where it has been trained on confused literature (foundations of QM, interpretations, fringe theories).
  • Finding genuine errors in published papers. Will agree with the paper.
  • Producing the one-line physical insight that turns a routine calculation into an interesting paper. That is the irreducibly human contribution.
  • Knowing when your draft is "done." Will iterate forever if asked. You decide.
The dangerous failure mode

The dangerous failure mode is not AI generating wrong physics — that is detectable. The dangerous failure mode is you delegating your understanding to AI. If you use AI to "explain" something you have not worked through yourself, you have not learned it. You have outsourced the cognition. When a referee asks you a follow-up question, you will not know the answer, and you will not know that you don't know.

The temptation is highest precisely when the material is hardest. The discipline required is to do the hard work yourself and then use AI to verify, summarize, or polish — never to replace.

Module 10Where it is going (5-year horizon)

Predictions are unreliable but the trajectory is clear in outline:

Near term (2026–2028)

  • AI becomes a universal research assistant. Drafting, refactoring, code-writing, literature search become AI-assisted by default.
  • Routine calculations (parameter scans, standard numerical methods) increasingly automated.
  • Most papers have some AI involvement; disclosure becomes standardized.
  • Some papers ship with their AI-generated code review.

Medium term (2028–2030)

  • AI begins to handle multi-step calculations reliably with verification. The Lean / Coq / formal-methods ecosystem matures; some theoretical results checked formally.
  • Literature synthesis at scale. "Show me every paper using the local Rindler horizon construction with f(R) gravity since 2010" becomes a working query.
  • Reviewers themselves use AI. The signal/noise of refereeing changes.
  • Specialized scientific AI (DeepMind-style applied physics agents) start producing papers — initially with strong human involvement, with the boundary increasingly hard to draw.
  • The skill of verifying AI output becomes a primary research skill.

Longer term (2030+)

  • The gap between human and AI contribution becomes harder to draw. Papers will list AI tools the way they list mathematical software now.
  • Some classes of research become AI-dominant (high-throughput parameter exploration, certain types of bug-finding in proofs). Others remain irreducibly human (taste, problem selection, conceptual leaps).
  • The premium on physical taste — knowing which problems matter — increases as routine output becomes cheap.
  • It is plausible that some research is produced by AI agents with human oversight; the institutional norms around this are not yet established.

The honest, uncomfortable summary

In five years, the routine content of most physics papers — calculations, literature reviews, exposition, code — will be substantially AI-produced and AI-verified. The hard, expensive thing will be physical insight: knowing which problem to attack, recognizing when an answer is real versus plausible-looking, identifying the connection that turns a calculation into an idea. The successful physicist of 2030 is one whose human contribution is concentrated in those upstream decisions, with the downstream execution accelerated by AI tools. The unsuccessful one is the person whose calculations and prose are AI-generated but whose understanding never caught up — they will produce mediocre work and not know that's what they are producing. — my honest reading, written in 2026 for the version of you that will be reading in 2030

Module 11You, specifically

You are a software engineer transitioning to physics. AI is your native tool. This is an advantage and a liability.

Your advantage

  • You are not intimidated by AI tools. You will deploy them faster than most early-career physicists.
  • You can write code that uses AI APIs in your research pipeline. PINN training, GP surrogate models, automated parameter scans — all native to you.
  • You can prompt AI more effectively than someone who has only used it for chat. You understand how to specify tasks unambiguously.

Your liability — and this is sharp

  • You have been asking AI (me) to do an unusual amount of the cognitive work in this workspace. The HTML files, the courses, the methodology — all generated. You have read them. You have not made them. The distinction matters.
  • If you outsource the understanding of physics to AI, you will not develop the physical intuition that distinguishes real work from plausible-looking work in five years. The intuition cannot be copied from training data; it has to be grown in your own head by sitting with hard problems.

Module 12Practical rules — what to do tomorrow

Concrete operational rules, distilled from the above.

  1. Compute first, ask later. Do the calculation by hand or in your own code before asking AI to explain or check it. If you do not have your own attempt, you cannot evaluate the AI's answer.
  2. Use AI for prose polish, not prose generation. Write a rough paragraph yourself. Ask AI to tighten it. Do not skip the rough draft.
  3. Verify every citation. AI hallucinates references with high confidence. Click through every arXiv link before citing.
  4. Use AI as a search tool, not as a source. "Are there papers on $X$?" is a fine query. "What does paper $Y$ say?" requires you to read paper $Y$ yourself.
  5. Disclose AI use. Per arXiv and journal policies. A footnote in the acknowledgments is sufficient: "We used [AI tool] for [specific tasks]."
  6. Maintain a "no AI" practice block. At least one block per day, do physics with paper, pen, and your own laptop's REPL — no AI assistant. This is where the intuition gets built.
  7. When stuck, sit with it longer before reaching for AI. The first half-hour of frustration is where understanding gets built. Skipping it short-circuits the learning.
  8. Treat AI output as a draft, never as final. Edit, rewrite, verify. If you can't tell whether a sentence in your paper is right, do not include it.

Module 13Pre-paper checklist — do you have a paper?

The decision tree, before committing months to a write-up:

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
1.  Can I state the result in one sentence?
        no  →  refine the question; you don't have a paper yet
        yes →  continue
                        ↓
2.  Is the result new? (search arXiv with 5 keyword combos)
        no  →  identify your distinct angle, OR drop project
        yes →  continue
                        ↓
3.  Is the calculation/argument sound, end to end,
    reproducible from the methods I will describe?
        no  →  finish the calculation before writing
        yes →  continue
                        ↓
4.  Does the result advance my field's question or
    open a new question?
        no  →  it may still be publishable but lower-tier;
               reconsider scope
        yes →  continue
                        ↓
5.  Can a hostile referee object to it without breaking
    the result?
        no  →  good — write
        yes →  fix the gap first, then write
                        ↓
6.  Can I imagine a future paper citing mine for
    something specific?
        no  →  the contribution is too vague
        yes →  WRITE THE PAPER
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Run this checklist for your centerpiece paper now. Honestly. Write the answers down, even one sentence per node. If you cannot pass node 1 or 2, the work to do first is not writing — it is sharpening the question. If you cannot pass node 3, the work is not writing — it is finishing the calculation. Only when all six pass do you commit to the months of write-up.

Closing — and what I'd actually do if I were you

Read Page 1976 (library access). Build the Schwarzschild benchmark in Python — by yourself, no AI for the physics, only for syntax debugging. Match Page's Table II to three digits. That is one week of work and produces something nobody else can take from you.

Then ask Hamming's questions weekly. If the greybody paper still feels worth doing, commit. If it doesn't, switch — but switch once, deliberately, after thinking, not in response to fatigue.

Do not let me write your physics. I cannot do it. The papers and courses I have made for you are useful, but the work that makes you a physicist is the work I cannot do — sitting with the hard derivation, alone, until it cracks.

Module 14What has actually shipped — evidence catalog (mid-2026)

The Part III modules above were too conservative. The honest 2026 catalog of AI-in-physics deliverables — only shipped results, with citations — is in this and the next four modules. Compiled by three parallel research agents in May 2026. Every citation can be verified by the reader.

14.1 The strongest single data point

AI Co-Scientist + Gemini Deep Think

Cosmic-string gravitational-wave power spectra (February 2026). Google's AI Co-Scientist (Feb 2025, multi-agent generate–debate–evolve over Gemini) combined with Deep Think tree search derived new exact analytical solutions for cosmic-string GW power spectra. The follow-up paper from Pushmeet Kohli's group is the most credible "AI did real theoretical physics" data point to date — a closed-form result, not just a fit. Read this as the bellwether.

References: AI Co-Scientist announcement, research.google/blog/accelerating-scientific-breakthroughs-with-an-ai-co-scientist/ (Feb 2025); follow-up cosmic-string paper Feb 2026.

QFT factorization theorem (arXiv, 5 Jan 2026). Matthew Schwartz supervised Claude Opus 4.5 through a factorization theorem in QFT. The paper was posted to arXiv on January 5, 2026. 14.4 Other shipped 2024–2026 results

  • Sakana AI Scientist v2 (arXiv:2504.08066, April 2025) — produced the first fully AI-generated paper accepted at an ICLR 2025 workshop (not main track). Cost ~$50–200 of compute per cycle. ML domain only; no physics analogue yet. github.com/SakanaAI/AI-Scientist-v2.
  • FermiNet excited states (Pfau et al., Science 385, eadn0137, Aug 2024; DOI:10.1126/science.adn0137) — neural-network quantum many-body excited-state computation, benchmark-beating on small molecules.
  • DINGO-BNS gravitational-wave Bayesian inference (Dax et al., PRL 134, 011403, Jan 2025; arXiv:2407.09602) — full BNS posterior in ~1 second, fast enough for pre-merger alerts. Classical pipelines cannot do this.
  • Calabi–Yau metric learning (Berglund et al., arXiv:2211.09801 + 2024 followups including arXiv:2401.04075) — ML-computed Ricci-flat metrics good enough for physical Yukawa couplings. String-landscape program: Constantin–Harvey–Lukas–Ruehle review at arXiv:2502.03245.
  • AlphaProof (DeepMind, Nature 2025; DOI:10.1038/s41586-025-09833-y) — IMO 2024 silver-medal level in Lean. Operates on Mathlib; physics is out of scope as long as Mathlib's Lorentzian geometry is absent.
  • AlphaEvolve (Google DeepMind, Nov 2025; arXiv:2511.02864) — Gemini-driven evolutionary coding agent. Recovered best-known solutions on 67 math problems, improved several.
  • Physics Supernova (Mengdi Wang, Princeton) — physics-tuned agent, 23.5/30 on the 2025 International Physics Olympiad. Olympiad ≠ research, but signals capability at undergraduate end-of-pipeline tasks.
  • Lattice QCD flow-based sampling (Albergo, Kanwar, Shanahan et al.) — normalizing-flow samplers (arXiv:2211.07541, PRD 106, 014514, etc.). Efficiency claims, no new physics yet.

14.5 What is documented as NOT shipped (gaps worth knowing)

Honest gaps
  • No verified symbolic-regression result that is genuinely new physics (rather than rediscovery or empirical fit) as of mid-2026. PySR and AI Feynman re-find Newton, Maxwell, Kepler reliably. The new-discovery list is essentially empty.
  • No physics analogue of Sakana's peer-reviewed AI-authored paper has cleared even workshop-level review.
  • No PRL, PRD, or Nature Physics editorial specifically setting policy on AI-assisted theoretical physics manuscripts (compare to Nature's general policy, Nature 613, 612, Jan 2023).

Methodology caveats — read these

Module 15Formal methods — Lean × AI × physics, where it actually stands

The formal-verification + AI-for-math arc is where mathematical physics gets touched first. Honest current state:

15.1 The first non-trivial QFT formalization

Constructive QFT in Lean 4 (March 2026)

arXiv:2603.15770. A Harvard/MIT team formalized the construction of free bosonic QFT in 4D Euclidean spacetime satisfying the Glimm–Jaffe / Osterwalder–Schrader axioms in Lean 4. Initially assumed Minlos' theorem, nuclearity of Schwartz space, and Goursat — all subsequently discharged. This is the first non-trivial constructive-QFT result in a proof assistant. The greybody-factor calculation is, in principle, within reach of a Mathlib + PhysLean stack once Lorentzian geometry matures.

15.2 Schwarzschild geometry, formally

The Schwarzschild curvature pipeline has been mechanized in Lean 4 (~16k lines, ResearchGate item 400459124, 2026): $g_{\mu\nu} \to \Gamma \to R^\rho{}_{\sigma\mu\nu} \to R_{\mu\nu} \to K$, verifying $R_{\mu\nu} = 0$ in the exterior and $K = 48 M^2/r^6$. This is a textbook coordinate computation, not a theorem about families of solutions.

15.3 What is NOT in Mathlib (for physicists)

  • Industrial-strength tensor calculus with abstract-index notation. There are partial implementations; nothing a physicist would actually use.
  • Lorentzian causality theory: achronal sets, trapped surfaces, focusing of null geodesics. None of the Penrose/Hawking machinery for singularity theorems exists.
  • Hawking's area theorem, Penrose's 1965 singularity theorem: not formalized in Lean, Coq, or Isabelle. Estimated 3–5 years minimum.
  • String-theory math: vertex operator algebras, modular tensor categories, characters of affine Lie algebras. Essentially absent from Mathlib. The biggest gap; unlikely to close on a 2–3 year timescale.

15.4 The LLM-prover stack (Lean-native)

  • Kimina-Prover — 92.2% on miniF2F (arXiv:2504.11354).
  • DeepSeek-Prover-V2 — top contender on competition math.
  • InternLM2-Step-Prover — Lean step-by-step proof generation.
  • Lean Copilot (arXiv:2404.12534) — IDE-integrated tactic suggestion.
  • Ax-Prover (arXiv:2510.12787, Oct 2025) — first agentic Lean workflow targeting research-level math and quantum physics.
  • Coverage: competition math and undergraduate real analysis. Physics is out of reach until Mathlib's geometry/topology catches up.

15.5 Physics-specific Lean tooling

  • PhysLean / PhysLib (arXiv:2405.08863, merging with Lean-QuantumInfo at github.com/leanprover-community/physlib) — has Wick's theorem, QM scaffolding, E&M bits.
  • Generalized Quantum Stein's Lemma formalized (arXiv:2510.08672).
  • Tooby-Smith 2025: used Lean to find a real error in a 2006 two-Higgs-doublet stability paper. This is the first documented Lean find of a published-but-wrong physics result.

15.6 Symbolic regression — the underrated near-term lever

  • PySR (Cranmer, arXiv:2305.01582; github.com/MilesCranmer/PySR) — now standard in LHC precision-observable pipelines (arXiv:2412.07839).
  • QDSR (arXiv:2503.19043) — 91.6% exact recovery on the AI-Feynman benchmark.
  • AI Poincaré (Liu–Tegmark, arXiv:2011.04698) — auto-discovers conserved quantities, including for the gravitational 3-body problem.

  • Bottom line: rediscovery, not discovery. The win is interpretability + acceleration. For your centerpiece paper, PySR on greybody-factor data could yield closed-form approximations that are publishable in their own right.

20.1 The information-paradox spine — Page → AMPS → ER=EPR → Islands

Reading 20.1.1 — Page 1993 (a)

D. N. Page, "Average entropy of a subsystem," PRL 71, 1291 (1993). arXiv: gr-qc/9305007. 10 pages.

What it does: proves the now-famous result that for a random pure state of a bipartite system $A \otimes B$ with $\dim A \le \dim B$, the average entanglement entropy of subsystem $A$ is very close to $\ln(\dim A)$ minus a tiny correction. Why it matters: the mathematical foundation of the Page curve. The "Page time" — the moment at which the radiation entropy starts to fall — comes directly from this result. Extract: the formula and its meaning; you will see it in every islands paper.

papers/bh_successors/Page1993a_AverageEntropy.pdf

Reading 20.1.2 — Page 1993 (b)

D. N. Page, "Information in black hole radiation," PRL 71, 3743 (1993). arXiv: hep-th/9306083. 13 pages.

What it does: applies the result of 20.1.1 to Hawking radiation. Argues that if BH evaporation is unitary, the entropy of the emitted radiation must follow a curve — flat, peak, then descending — which is now called the Page curve. Why it matters: the entire islands program is the attempt to derive this curve from semiclassical gravity. Without this paper, there is no "information paradox" as we mean it today. Extract: the qualitative shape of the curve and its physical interpretation. The crux of every modern paper.

papers/bh_successors/Page1993b_InfoInRadiation.pdf

Reading 20.1.3 — Susskind–Thorlacius–Uglum 1993

L. Susskind, L. Thorlacius, J. Uglum, "The stretched horizon and black hole complementarity," PRD 48, 3743 (1993). arXiv: hep-th/9306069. 48 pages.

What it does: the original statement of black hole complementarity — observers outside vs. observers falling in see incompatible but locally consistent descriptions. The "stretched horizon" is the membrane at $\sim$ Planck distance outside the event horizon where the outside observer's degrees of freedom live. Why it matters: the whole edifice of how high-energy physicists think about BH information traces back here. AMPS 2013 is direct rebuttal; ER=EPR is the modern resolution. Extract: the principle, the stretched horizon picture, the formalism.

papers/bh_successors/STU1993_StretchedHorizon.pdf

Reading 20.1.4 — Hayden–Preskill 2007

P. Hayden, J. Preskill, "Black holes as mirrors," JHEP 0709 (2007) 120. arXiv: 0708.4025. 18 pages.

What it does: shows that if you throw a qubit into an already-old (past Page time) black hole, you can recover it from the Hawking radiation extraordinarily quickly — within a scrambling time. Why it matters: a foundational result in quantum-information-applied-to-BH-physics. The "Hayden–Preskill decoding problem" is now a standard probe of holographic models. Extract: the protocol; the scrambling-time prediction.

papers/bh_successors/HaydenPreskill2007_BHMirrors.pdf

Reading 20.1.5 — AMPS 2013 (Firewalls)

A. Almheiri, D. Marolf, J. Polchinski, J. Sully, "Black holes: complementarity or firewalls?", JHEP 02 (2013) 062. arXiv: 1207.3123. 24 pages.

What it does: argues that BH complementarity is inconsistent with unitarity, entanglement monogamy, and the equivalence principle all at once for old black holes. The proposed resolution is a "firewall" at the horizon — a wall of high-energy quanta destroying any infalling observer. Why it matters: the paper that broke open the modern information paradox debate; everything from ER=EPR through islands is partly a response. Extract: the trilemma argument; the precise statement of the contradiction.

papers/bh_successors/AMPS2013_Firewalls.pdf

Reading 20.1.6 — Maldacena–Susskind 2013 (ER = EPR)

J. Maldacena, L. Susskind, "Cool horizons for entangled black holes," Fortsch. Phys. 61, 781 (2013). arXiv: 1306.0533. 49 pages.

What it does: proposes the conjecture ER = EPR: two black holes connected by an Einstein–Rosen bridge (wormhole) are equivalent to two black holes in an entangled (EPR) state. Generalization: entanglement is geometric connectivity. Why it matters: the modern face of holography. Directly relevant to your project: the metric as emergent from entanglement (the deep Jacobson connection). Extract: the conjecture, the supporting examples, the implications for the firewall paradox.

papers/bh_successors/MaldacenaSusskind2013_ER_EPR.pdf

Reading 20.1.7 — AMMZ 2019 (Page curve from semiclassical geometry)

A. Almheiri, R. Mahajan, J. Maldacena, Y. Zhao, "The Page curve of Hawking radiation from semiclassical geometry," JHEP 03 (2020) 149. arXiv: 1908.10996. 22 pages.

What it does: a companion to Penington 2019 and AEMM 2019; derives the descending part of the Page curve from a semiclassical bulk calculation using the "quantum extremal surface" prescription. Why it matters: the cleanest version of the islands argument. Extract: the quantum extremal surface formula; the role of replica wormholes.

papers/bh_successors/AMMZ2019_PageCurveSemiclassical.pdf

Reading 20.1.8 — PSSY 2019 (Replica wormholes)

G. Penington, S. H. Shenker, D. Stanford, Z. Yang, "Replica wormholes and the black hole interior," JHEP 03 (2022) 205. arXiv: 1911.11977. 79 pages.

What it does: derives the islands formula via the replica trick, including the contribution of wormhole-like saddles in the gravitational path integral. Why it matters: the technical workhorse paper that made islands respectable. Long; only the central calculation (and §1–2) is necessary on first pass. Extract: the replica method as applied to BH, and how wormholes restore the Page curve.

papers/bh_successors/PSSY2019_ReplicaWormholes.pdf

Reading 20.1.9 — AHMST 2020 (the comprehensive review)

A. Almheiri, T. Hartman, J. Maldacena, E. Shaghoulian, A. Tajdini, "The entropy of Hawking radiation," Rev. Mod. Phys. 93, 035002 (2021). arXiv: 2006.06872. 47 pages.

20.3 The Jacobson program — beyond 1995

Reading 20.3.1 — Eling–Guedens–Jacobson 2006

Why it matters: identifies precisely where the 1995 program assumes equilibrium, and what the "non-equilibrium thermodynamics of spacetime" looks like.
